How Does a Recommendation Algorithm Actually Work?

Every time Netflix suggests a show or Amazon says "you might also like," an algorithm is running in the background. Understanding how it works is key to understanding AI's most powerful marketing application.

Recommendation algorithms are everywhere. They decide what shows up in your YouTube feed, what products Amazon highlights, what songs Spotify adds to your playlist, and what posts appear first in your Instagram feed. They're the most visible — and most financially significant — application of AI in marketing today.

Amazon's recommendation engine is estimated to drive about 35% of total revenue. Netflix says 80% of what people watch comes from recommendations. These aren't small improvements — they're fundamental to how these businesses operate.

How the Algorithm Actually Works

Under the hood, recommendation algorithms follow a series of steps to turn raw data into a ranked list of things you're likely to enjoy.

1

Collect Signals

Every action you take — clicking, watching, buying, rating, pausing, searching — is recorded. Even what you don't click is data. The algorithm logs how long you hover over something before moving on.

2

Weight the Signals

Not all signals are equal. Finishing a movie is a stronger signal than starting one. A 5-star rating is stronger than a browse. The algorithm learns which signals are the best predictors of what you'll actually enjoy.

3

Find Your "Taste Cluster"

The algorithm compares your behavior to millions of other users. It finds people whose past behavior closely matches yours — your "taste cluster" — and assumes that what they liked, you'll probably like too. This is called collaborative filtering.

4

Analyze Content Features

The algorithm also analyzes the items themselves — genres, directors, actors, themes, style. If you loved three movies with similar visual styles, it will surface more movies with that style even if nobody told it to. This is content-based filtering.

5

Score and Rank

Every item gets a predicted "enjoyment score" for you specifically — a probability that you'll like it. The algorithm ranks everything and surfaces the top items, adjusting in real time as your behavior continues to update the model.

Two Main Types of Filtering

👥
Collaborative Filtering
Based on the behavior of users similar to you. "People who liked what you liked also liked this." It works across categories — even if you've never shown interest in something directly.
💡 Amazon: "Customers who bought this also bought..." — finding patterns across millions of similar shoppers to predict what you'll want next.
🎭
Content-Based Filtering
Based on the features of items you've liked. "You liked items with these characteristics, so here are more items with similar characteristics." Doesn't require other users.
💡 Spotify: After you listen to songs with similar BPM, key, and instrumentation, it recommends tracks with matching audio features — even ones no one has told you about.

Most modern recommendation systems use both — combining collaborative and content signals to produce better recommendations than either approach alone.

A recommendation algorithm isn't trying to read your mind. It's doing something more interesting: it's finding patterns in what millions of people with similar tastes have done — and using that collective intelligence to predict your next move.

Why This Matters for Marketers

As a marketer, understanding recommendation algorithms means understanding how to get your products or content surfaced by them. Quality signals (high ratings, long watch/read times, high completion rates) tell algorithms your content is worth recommending. Bad signals (high skip rates, quick exits) suppress you. Modern marketing isn't just about reaching customers — it's about earning algorithmic distribution.

📌 What You Just Learned

Article Summary

Recommendation algorithms collect signals from everything you do — clicks, watches, ratings, searches, and even what you don't click — then weight those signals to understand your preferences.

Collaborative filtering finds users similar to you and recommends what they liked. Content-based filtering recommends items with features similar to ones you've enjoyed. Most systems combine both.

The business impact is enormous: Amazon's recommendation engine drives ~35% of revenue, Netflix recommendations drive 80% of viewing — these algorithms are not features, they're core business infrastructure.

For marketers, earning algorithmic distribution — creating content that generates strong signals — is now as important as traditional advertising in reaching customers.
