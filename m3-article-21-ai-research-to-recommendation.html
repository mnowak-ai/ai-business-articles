How to Turn AI Research Into a Business Recommendation

AI gives you data and insights. But data and insights aren't a recommendation. Transforming research into something a decision-maker can actually act on is a distinct skill — and one of the most valuable things you can bring to any organization.

There's a gap that catches many people off guard when they start using AI for professional research. AI can produce an impressive research brief — market data, competitive landscape, trend analysis, risk factors. But when someone asks "so what should we do?", the research alone doesn't answer that question.

A business recommendation is more than research — it's a specific, justified course of action that translates insights into something actionable. The lesson video showed this using Nike: AI can analyze shoe endorsement options and compare brands — but someone still has to say "based on this, here's what I recommend and why." That someone is you.

The 5-Step Framework: Research to Recommendation

This framework gives you a repeatable process for turning any AI research output into a well-structured business recommendation.

1
Step 1
Crystallize the Question

Before anything else, make sure you're answering the right question. Many recommendations fail because they answer a slightly different question than the one the decision-maker actually needs answered. State the question clearly before you start.

📌 Not: "What does the market look like?" → But: "Should we enter the Southeast Asian market in Q1 next year, and if so, which country first?"
2
Step 2
Extract the 3 Most Relevant Findings

From all your AI research, identify the three data points or insights that most directly answer the specific question. Don't include everything — include what's most relevant. A recommendation buried under 15 findings is harder to act on than one grounded in three sharp ones.

📌 From a 30-page AI market brief, pull: (1) market size/growth, (2) most relevant competitive gap, (3) single biggest risk or opportunity.
3
Step 3
Add Context AI Doesn't Have

This is where human judgment earns its place. What does AI not know about your company, your team, your relationships, your timing, your budget, or your strategic priorities that would affect what this research means? Layer that context in before making the call.

📌 The data supports entering Vietnam. But: your team has no Southeast Asia experience, and your CEO has a strong relationship in Singapore. That context changes the recommendation.
4
Step 4
Make a Clear, Specific Recommendation

State your recommendation as a specific, actionable direction — not a vague suggestion. "We should consider exploring this" is not a recommendation. "I recommend we launch in Singapore in Q2 with a $500K initial budget" is a recommendation.

📌 Weak: "The data suggests international expansion may be worth considering." Strong: "I recommend we prioritize Singapore for a Q2 pilot, leveraging our CEO's existing partnership contact."
5
Step 5
Define the Next Action

A recommendation without a next step stays on a slide forever. Close the loop: what specifically should happen next, who should own it, and by when? This turns a recommendation into a decision trigger.

📌 "If leadership approves this direction, the next step is: [Person] schedules exploratory conversations with the Singapore partnership contact by [Date] and reports back to the team by [Date]."

The difference between someone who "found interesting research" and someone who "moved a decision forward" is the recommendation layer. Data without a recommendation is reading material. Data with a recommendation is a business contribution.

From the lesson video: Nike is analyzing shoe endorsement options. AI does the research. See how each step of the framework plays out in this real business scenario.

🎮 Nike Endorsement Scenario

Step 1
The Question: Which athlete to endorse?
+
🤖 AI Input
Analyzing endorsement options requires comparing brand alignment, audience reach, performance metrics, market positioning, and contract risk factors across athlete candidates.
✅ Human Clarification
The real question is more specific: "Given our goal to increase market share among 18–25 runners, which athlete endorsement will have the highest ROI over 3 years?" Clarifying this focuses everything that follows.
Step 2
AI Research: Comparing the candidates
+
🤖 AI Findings
Athlete A: 2.8M Instagram followers, 73% 18–25 audience, 4 major brand partnerships, no controversy history, performance: ranked top 10 globally. Athlete B: 5.1M followers, 45% 18–25 audience, 2 partnerships, minor controversy 18 months ago, ranked top 5 globally. Athlete C: 900K followers, 81% 18–25 audience, no existing partnerships (clean slate), ranked top 20, rising trajectory.
✅ Human Filtering
The 3 most relevant data points for the specific question: audience age match, controversy risk, and partnership exclusivity. AI found all three — but ranking their importance for Nike's specific situation requires human judgment about the brand's current risk appetite and goals.
Step 3
Adding context AI doesn't have
+
🤖 AI Limitation
AI doesn't know: Nike's current internal controversy tolerance after recent PR situations; that Athlete B's "minor controversy" involved a topic highly sensitive to Nike's target demographic specifically; or that Nike's marketing team has a pre-existing relationship with Athlete C's management agency.
✅ Human Context Added
This changes the picture significantly. Athlete B's controversy risk is higher than the data suggests given audience sensitivity. Athlete C's relationship advantage offsets the smaller follower count — and the "clean slate" makes the partnership story easier to tell.
Step 4
The Recommendation
+
🤖 AI Suggestion
"Based on data, Athlete A presents the best balance of audience fit, performance credibility, and low risk. Athlete C is a high-upside alternative worth considering."
✅ Human Recommendation
"I recommend Athlete C as the primary endorsement. The 81% target-audience match is the highest of the three. The existing agency relationship reduces negotiation time and cost. The rising trajectory creates a partnership narrative that grows with the athlete — more compelling than endorsing an already-established star. Secondary recommendation: explore a smaller co-partnership with Athlete A to hedge performance risk."
Step 5
Next Action
+
🤖 AI Output
AI can generate a list of potential next steps but cannot determine which should be prioritized or who owns them.
✅ Human Next Step
"If leadership approves this direction, the next step is: Sarah [Marketing VP] reaches out to Athlete C's agency contact within 5 business days to gauge contract appetite. If initial conversations are positive, present a formal proposal to the brand committee by [Date]."

Weak vs. Strong Recommendations: Side by Side

❌ Weak Recommendation
"The research suggests that entering the European market could be beneficial given current trends. There may be opportunities worth exploring, though more analysis might be needed before making a final decision."
✅ Strong Recommendation
"I recommend entering Germany as our first European market in Q3. The data shows the highest addressable market ($2.1B) and lowest competitive density of any EU country. The next step is engaging a local distribution partner — I've identified three candidates to evaluate."

Notice the difference: the weak version hedges, generalizes, and defers. The strong version commits to a specific direction, cites the evidence, and closes with an action. Both could come from the same AI research — the difference is entirely in what the human author adds.

🎯

The Recommendation Is Where Your Value Lies

AI can produce the research brief. Anyone with access to the same tools can produce the same brief. What differentiates you is what you add on top: the right question, the most relevant findings selected from the noise, the context from knowing your organization, the specific recommendation, and the actionable next step. That layer is irreducibly human — and it's what organizations actually need from people who use AI well.

📌 What You Just Learned

Article Summary

The 5-step framework: crystallize the question → extract 3 key findings → add context AI doesn't have → make a specific recommendation → define the next action.

The human value-add lives at Steps 3 and 4: adding organizational context AI can't access, and making a specific, committed recommendation rather than hedging with "we may want to consider."

A strong recommendation is specific, justified by evidence, aware of context, and closes with a clear next action and owner. A weak recommendation hedges and defers — leaving the decision-maker with more reading but no clearer direction.

The recommendation layer is where you differentiate yourself from someone who just ran the same AI prompts. It's the irreducibly human contribution that turns research into organizational value.
