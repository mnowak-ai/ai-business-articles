Lesson 3.2 · Using AI for Research

Writing Research Prompts That Actually Get Results

The quality of what AI gives you is almost entirely determined by the quality of what you ask. Learning to write strong research prompts is the single highest-leverage AI skill you can develop.

Here's the uncomfortable truth most people discover after their first few AI interactions: the tool is only as good as the person using it. Give AI a vague, low-effort question and you get a vague, low-effort answer. Give it a precisely engineered prompt and you get research-quality output that would take a junior analyst hours to produce.

Prompt engineering — the skill of crafting specific, structured instructions for AI — is what separates mediocre AI users from people who actually get value out of the technology. And for business research specifically, there are five components that every strong prompt should include.

Structure your response with: market size, top 3 competitors with their pricing models, the most underserved customer segment, and 2 key risks. A well-built research prompt is like a good job description: the more specific and complete it is, the better the output you'll get from whoever — or whatever — is executing it.

Research Need: Understand the streaming music market
❌ WEAK: "Tell me about the music streaming market."
✅ STRONG: "Give me a research brief on the U.S. music streaming market. I work in business development at an independent record label evaluating distribution strategy. Include: total market size in 2024, the top 4 platforms with their royalty rate structures, which artist tier (independent vs. major label) each platform favors, and what the biggest shifts in the market have been in the last 2 years. Use bullet points and keep it under 500 words."
Why the strong version works: It defines the audience (independent record label), specifies exactly what information is needed (royalty rates, platform preferences), adds a time constraint (last 2 years), requests a format (bullet points), and sets a length limit. The weak version gives AI no direction — it could write anything from a Wikipedia summary to a 5,000 word essay.
Research Need: Understand competitors in the project management software space
❌ WEAK: "Who are the competitors in project management software?"
✅ STRONG: "I'm evaluating whether to build a project management tool specifically for marketing agencies. Compare the top 5 existing tools (Asana, Monday.com, Notion, ClickUp, Basecamp) on: pricing per user per month, standout feature for creative teams, most common user complaint from reviews, and whether they have an agency-specific plan. Format as a comparison table."
Why the strong version works: It names the specific competitors to compare (no ambiguity), specifies the exact data points to extract for each, anchors the analysis to a specific use case (marketing agencies), and requests a structured output format (comparison table). The weak version will produce a list of company names and vague descriptions that you'd still have to research further yourself.
Research Need: Understand customer behavior in e-commerce
❌ WEAK: "What do customers want when shopping online?"
✅ STRONG: "I manage a DTC skincare brand targeting women aged 28–40. Based on current consumer behavior research, identify the top 5 factors that influence whether a first-time visitor converts to a buyer on a skincare e-commerce site. For each factor, give one specific action I could take to improve it. Include any relevant statistics from the last 3 years."
Why the strong version works: It specifies the industry (skincare), the customer (women 28–40), the exact question (conversion factors), the actionable output format (one specific action per factor), and a recency constraint (last 3 years). The weak version will produce generic e-commerce advice that could apply to any industry and gives you nothing actionable.

Prompts Are Conversations, Not One-Shots

One of the biggest mistakes new AI users make is treating each prompt as a single transaction. In reality, the best research sessions are iterative — you start with a broad framework prompt, evaluate the output, then follow up with more targeted questions. "Go deeper on the third competitor" or "reformat this as a table" or "what did you leave out?" are all valid follow-ups that progressively sharpen the research. Think of it less like a search engine and more like working with a research assistant you can direct in real time.

📌 Article Summary

What You Just Learned

A strong research prompt has 5 components: role/persona, topic/scope, context/purpose, output format, and constraints. Missing any one of these degrades the output quality.

The difference between a weak and strong prompt isn't just quality — it's whether the output is actually usable for a business decision. Weak prompts generate content. Strong prompts generate insights.

Specifying format (table, bullets, sections), length, and recency constraints dramatically improves the usability of AI research responses.

Treat AI research as a conversation — follow up, refine, and redirect. The best research sessions are iterative, not one-shot queries.
