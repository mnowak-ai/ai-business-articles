Lesson 5.3 · Meeting Notes & Summaries

The Anatomy of Good Meeting Notes

Meeting notes are not a transcript of everything said. They're a structured record of what was decided, what was committed, and what still needs resolution. Understanding the difference is the foundation of everything AI meeting tools do.

Notes ≠ Transcription

Most people think good meeting notes are comprehensive — the more captured, the better. This is wrong. A complete transcript of every word spoken in a meeting is almost useless for the people who weren't there, and only marginally useful for the people who were.

What people actually need from meeting notes is specific: What was decided? Who's doing what? What's still unresolved? A great set of meeting notes answers all three questions clearly in under two minutes of reading. That takes discipline — or well-configured AI.

Nobody reads meeting transcripts. Everybody needs meeting decisions. Those are not the same document.

The 5 Elements Every Good Set of Notes Contains

1

Header: Meeting context

Date, time, attendees, purpose, and facilitator. This is the metadata that makes notes searchable and useful three months later.

Example: "Product Review · May 14 · Attendees: Sarah (DM), Marcus, Priya (facilitator), Jordan (notes) · Goal: Decide Q3 priorities"
2

Key Discussion Points (not everything said)

A brief summary of the major topics covered — not a word-for-word transcript. One or two sentences per agenda item capturing the substance of the discussion.

Example: "Team debated two approaches for the checkout flow redesign: Option A (minimal changes, faster delivery) vs Option B (full redesign, 6-week timeline)."
3

Decisions Made — explicit and attributed

Every formal decision captured clearly, with who made it. The word "decided" should appear explicitly. Not "we discussed Option B" — "Sarah decided to proceed with Option B."

Example: "✅ DECIDED: Proceed with Option A for the checkout flow. Owner: Marcus. Rationale: faster delivery to meet Q3 revenue target."
4

Action Items — task / owner / deadline

Every commitment made during the meeting, written in the same three-part format: what needs to happen, who owns it, by when. No exceptions.

Example: "⬜ Marcus: Draft technical spec for Option A checkout flow → Due Friday May 17"
5

Open Questions & Parking Lot

Issues raised that couldn't be resolved in the meeting — flagged for follow-up. This section prevents important topics from disappearing into the "we'll get to that eventually" void.

Example: "❓ OPEN: Legal review of new checkout copy needed before dev can start. Marcus to follow up with Legal by EOD Thursday."

See the Difference: Bad Notes vs. Good Notes

Same meeting. Two very different records of what happened:

❌ Bad Meeting Notes
May 14 meeting notes: - talked about checkout flow - Marcus and Sarah had different views - went back and forth on options - product launch stuff - someone said Friday deadline - more to discuss next week
Problems: No decision captured. No owner named. No deadline is actionable. Impossible to know what was agreed. Two people will leave with two interpretations. "More to discuss" buries the open question.
✅ Good Meeting Notes
Product Review · May 14 · 10:00 AM Attendees: Sarah (VP, DM), Marcus (Eng), Priya (facilitator), Jordan (notes) Goal: Decide Q3 checkout flow approach Discussion: Team reviewed Option A (minimal, 3 weeks) vs Option B (full redesign, 6 weeks). Marcus flagged Option B carries integration risk. Sarah weighed against Q3 revenue deadline. ✅ DECIDED: Proceed with Option A. Owner: Marcus | Rationale: Q3 deadline priority ⬜ ACTION: Marcus — technical spec for Option A Due: Friday May 17 ❓ OPEN: Legal review of checkout copy required Marcus following up with Legal by Thursday EOD
This works because: decision is explicit, attributed, and rationale-noted. Action item has owner and deadline. Open question has a follow-up owner and date. Readable in 90 seconds.

Weekly Product Sync — June 3, 2025
Facilitator: Priya | Note-taker: Jordan (+ Otter.ai backup) | Duration: 42 min
📋 Attendees & Context Why this matters
Sarah Chen (VP Product, Decision-Maker), Marcus Williams (Sr. Engineer), Priya Patel (PM, Facilitator), Jordan Lee (Associate PM, Notes)
Goal: Finalize Q3 feature priorities and unblock design phase
The header tells anyone reading these notes exactly who was there, who had decision authority, and what the meeting was trying to accomplish. Without this, notes read as a series of statements with no author or authority attached. Three months later, this header is the only way to reconstruct why a particular decision was made.
💬 Discussion Summary What to include
Team reviewed 5 proposed features for Q3. Debate centered on Feature 3 (notification overhaul) vs Feature 4 (performance improvements). Marcus noted Feature 3 requires new infrastructure work, estimated at 4 weeks. Sarah raised concern about user churn data suggesting performance is higher priority.
Discussion summary captures the substance of the debate — not every word spoken. A good note-taker identifies the key argument on each side and preserves it. This serves two purposes: people who weren't there understand the reasoning behind the decision, and people who were there can verify the record if memories diverge later. Two to four sentences per major topic is usually right.
✅ Decisions Why explicit attribution matters
DECIDED: Feature 4 (performance) prioritized over Feature 3 (notifications) for Q3. Sarah's decision, based on churn data. Feature 3 moved to Q4 backlog.
DECIDED: Design phase to begin June 10 pending technical spec completion. Priya to confirm readiness by June 8.
Every decision needs three things: what was decided (explicit, not implied), who decided it (named), and why (brief rationale). "We agreed" is a trap — if Sarah and Marcus leave the meeting with different memories, "we agreed" doesn't help. "Sarah decided, based on churn data" is a record that can be referenced. This also creates accountability: if the decision turns out to be wrong, the reasoning can be revisited.
⬜ Action Items The three-part rule
⬜ Marcus: Complete technical spec for Feature 4 → DUE June 7
⬜ Priya: Confirm design team readiness → DUE June 8
⬜ Jordan: Send these notes to all attendees + Slack #product-team → DUE Today EOD
The three-part rule for action items: Task (what happens), Owner (one person's name — not "the team"), Deadline (specific date — not "soon" or "next week"). All three parts are required. A task with no owner never gets done. A task with no deadline always waits for the next meeting. AI meeting tools extract these automatically — but they need the spoken commitment to be clear enough in the discussion to extract correctly.
❓ Open Questions The parking lot
❓ Should Feature 3 be fully scoped before Q4 planning? Marcus to assess effort estimate — no deadline set yet.
❓ User research data referenced by Sarah: Jordan to locate original report and share in Slack before next sync.
Open questions are the things raised in the meeting that couldn't be resolved. Recording them prevents two failure modes: (1) someone assumes it was decided when it wasn't, and (2) the topic is forgotten entirely until it resurfaces as a crisis. Each open question should ideally have a follow-up owner even if it doesn't have a deadline yet.
🧠 Quick Check
After a meeting, Jordan writes this action item: "Marcus — look into the API thing." What is wrong with this action item and how should it be fixed?
📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Good meeting notes are not transcripts. They answer three questions: What was decided? Who's doing what? What's still unresolved? Everything else is optional.

The 5 elements: Header, Discussion Summary, Decisions (explicit + attributed), Action Items (task / owner / deadline), and Open Questions. All five are required for notes to be useful.

AI meeting tools apply exactly this structure automatically — but they work best when the spoken meeting is clear enough to extract decisions and commitments from. Structure in the meeting creates structure in the notes.
