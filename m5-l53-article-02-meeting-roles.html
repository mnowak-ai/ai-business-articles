Meeting Roles: Who Does What

A meeting without defined roles is a conversation. A conversation can be valuable — but it rarely produces documented decisions and clear ownership. Before you can take great notes, you need to understand the structure that makes notes meaningful.

Why Roles Matter Before Notes

Meeting notes don't exist in a vacuum. They're a record of decisions, discussions, and commitments made by people in specific roles. Without knowing who was in which role, it's impossible to capture a meeting accurately — you can write down what was said, but you can't write down what it meant, who owns what came out of it, or whether it was a final decision or a preliminary discussion.

What Good vs. Bad Role Assignment Looks Like

❌ Meeting without defined roles
🌀 Everyone talks at once, conversation drifts from topic to topic
🌀 Nobody is capturing what's being said — it's all in people's heads
🌀 Discussion of a decision goes 35 minutes without resolution because nobody is managing time
🌀 Meeting ends without clarity on what was decided or who does what next
🌀 Three people leave with three different interpretations of the outcome
🌀 Notes (if they exist) are a scattered bullet list nobody can parse later
✅ Meeting with clear roles assigned in advance
✅ Facilitator opens with agenda and time checks — discussion stays on track
✅ Note-taker (or AI tool) captures decisions and commitments as they happen
✅ Facilitator calls the decision at minute 15: "We're going with Option B — Marcus, are you confirming that?"
✅ Decision-maker is present and gives explicit sign-off before the topic closes
✅ Action items captured in real time: task / owner / deadline — no ambiguity
✅ Notes distributed within 24 hours — shared record everyone can verify

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Four roles make meetings work: Facilitator (keeps things on track), Note-taker (captures decisions and commitments), Decision-maker (has authority to decide), and Participants (come prepared, contribute on-topic).

AI meeting tools automate the note-taking function — but they don't replace the human accountability to review outputs, verify accuracy, and own follow-through.

In small teams, one person may fill multiple roles. What matters is that each role is explicitly assigned — not assumed — before the meeting begins.
