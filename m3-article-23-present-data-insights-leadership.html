Lesson 3.4 · Supporting Decisions

How to Present Data-Backed Insights to Your Boss or Team

Great research that isn't communicated well doesn't move decisions forward. The lesson video showed the formula: your recommendation, your reasoning, your next step. This article builds that skill in full.

You've done the AI research. You've verified the key facts. You've added context and formed a recommendation. Now comes the moment that actually matters: communicating it to the people who need to act on it.

Here's what most people get wrong: they present the research instead of the insight. They walk through all the data they gathered — every finding, every source, every nuance — instead of leading with what it means and what should happen next. Decision-makers don't need to hear all your research. They need to hear what it adds up to.

The Lesson 3.4 slide said it clearly: your recommendation + your reasoning + your next step. Three parts. In that order. Let's break each one down.

The 3-Part Structure That Always Works

📊
Your Recommendation
Lead with the answer

State your recommended direction first — not your research, not the background, not the problem. The answer. Decision-makers are time-constrained; give them the conclusion they can immediately evaluate.

✅ "I recommend we expand into the German market in Q3 with an initial budget of €400K."
🔍
Your Reasoning
Back it up with data + context

Give the 2–3 most compelling reasons from your research — supported by data — plus any relevant context that shaped your judgment. This is where the research appears, but selectively, not exhaustively.

✅ "Germany has the EU's highest addressable market ($3.1B), lowest competitive density of any target country, and we have an existing distribution partnership there."
🚀
Your Next Step
Propose a clear, actionable plan

Close with a specific, owned next action — not "we should explore this further" but exactly what should happen, who should do it, and by when. This turns the presentation into a decision trigger.

✅ "If approved, Marcus will contact our Munich distribution partner by Friday to confirm interest and timeline. We'll report back at the next leadership meeting."

Lead with the recommendation, not the journey. Your audience didn't ask to watch you do research — they asked for your conclusion. Give them that first, then support it.

Before & After: The Same Insight, Two Presentations

❌ Data Dump — Avoid This
"So I spent the last week looking into our pricing strategy and found a lot of interesting things. The market data shows that our prices are about 15% above the category average, and when I looked at the competitive landscape, I found that three of our main competitors have been lowering prices over the last two quarters. I also looked at our customer survey data and it seems like price sensitivity is higher than we thought, especially in the 25–35 age bracket. There's also some correlation between price and churn rate that might be worth looking into further. I think this could potentially be something worth discussing, though obviously there are trade-offs to consider..."
Problems: Buries the insight under process narration. No clear recommendation. Ends with "worth discussing" — not actionable. Leadership leaves the meeting without a direction.
✅ Insight-First — Do This
"I recommend a targeted 12% price reduction in our core product tier for customers aged 25–35. Here's why: our prices are 15% above category average, three competitors have cut prices this quarter, and our survey data shows price sensitivity is highest in the 25–35 segment — which is also our highest churn group. A 12% reduction in this tier would put us at category parity where it matters most while protecting margins on higher-value segments. Next step: if the team agrees on direction, Finance runs the P&L impact model this week, and we bring a decision to leadership by Thursday."
What changed: Led with the recommendation. Three supporting reasons, not ten. Specific price point and segment. Clear next step with owner and deadline.

Acknowledge What You Don't Know

Strong presentations include honest uncertainty. If your recommendation is based on data that might be incomplete, or if there's a scenario where your recommendation would change, say so. "This recommendation assumes our supply chain can support a 30% volume increase — if that's not confirmed, the timeline changes" is far stronger than presenting with false confidence. Decision-makers respect intellectual honesty and will trust your judgment more, not less, when you flag your own caveats proactively.

📌 What You Just Learned

Article Summary

The 3-part structure: recommendation first (the answer) → reasoning (2–3 data-supported reasons) → next step (specific owner and deadline). In that order, every time.

The most common mistake is presenting research instead of insight — walking through all the data rather than leading with what it adds up to. Decision-makers need the conclusion first, supported by selective evidence.

A strong next step includes a specific action, a named owner, and a deadline. "We should explore this" is not a next step — it's a deferral.

Acknowledging honest uncertainty in your presentation builds trust — proactively flagging caveats and conditions is a sign of intellectual rigor, not weakness.
