How to Write a Prompt That Actually Gets Good Results

The output you get from an AI tool is only as good as the input you give it. Prompt writing is the single most important skill for getting value out of AI — and most people are doing it wrong.

Most people approach AI like a search engine — they type a few words and hope for the best. "Write me an email." "Make a report." "Help with this." And then they're disappointed when the output is generic, misses the point, or doesn't sound like them.

The problem isn't the AI. It's the prompt. A prompt is a set of instructions — and the quality of your instructions determines the quality of your output. Treat AI like a very capable new team member who needs clear direction, not a magic box that reads your mind.

The Five Elements of a Great Prompt

Every strong prompt contains some combination of these five elements. The more you include, the better and more targeted the result. Hover over the highlighted parts to see which element each one is.

Anatomy of a Strong Prompt
Act as a professional business writing coach and write a follow-up email to a potential client we met at a conference last Tuesday. The client is the VP of Marketing at a mid-size retail company. We discussed helping them improve their internal communication strategy. They seemed interested but said they needed to check with their team before committing. The email should be 3–4 short paragraphs, no bullet points. Use a warm but professional tone — not pushy, just following up naturally.

You don't need all five every time — but the more relevant context you provide, the more targeted and useful the output will be. For a simple request ("write me a one-line email saying I'll be 5 minutes late to the call"), brevity is fine. For complex professional documents, all five elements matter.

Weak vs. Strong Prompts — Side by Side

Let's look at real before/after examples across different document types. Notice how the stronger prompt gives the AI everything it needs to produce something actually useful.

📧 Email — Following Up After No Response
❌ Weak Prompt
"Write a follow-up email."
Result: A generic, one-size-fits-all email that could be for anyone in any situation. Requires heavy rewriting to be useful.
✅ Strong Prompt
"Write a professional follow-up email to a client who hasn't responded to a proposal I sent 10 days ago. The proposal was for a $15,000 website redesign. Tone should be friendly and non-pushy — I want to check in without sounding desperate. Keep it under 100 words."
Result: A targeted, appropriately toned email you can send with minor edits.
📋 Memo — Policy Change Announcement
❌ Weak Prompt
"Write a memo about the new parking policy."
Result: A generic memo template with placeholder information. The AI has no idea what the policy actually says.
✅ Strong Prompt
"Write a formal memo from HR to all 80 employees at our company announcing a new parking policy. Key details: parking will now require a permit starting March 1st; permits are free and available from reception; unnumbered spots are first-come-first-served; visitors must use the visitor lot on the east side. Include the action required (pick up permit by Feb 28) and a contact for questions."
Result: A complete, ready-to-send memo with all the relevant information in the right format.
📊 Report — Executive Summary
❌ Weak Prompt
"Write an executive summary of our Q3 results."
Result: A template with blanks or made-up numbers. The AI doesn't know your results.
✅ Strong Prompt
"Write a 200-word executive summary for our Q3 business report. Key facts: revenue was $2.4M (up 14% vs Q2), customer acquisition cost dropped 22%, we launched two new product features, and our NPS score improved from 41 to 58. The audience is the board of directors. Lead with the strongest result, then briefly cover each key metric. Tone: confident and data-focused."
Result: A polished executive summary built on your real data that's ready for leadership review.

The quality of your prompt is the quality of your thinking made visible. If you're vague with AI, it's because you're vague about what you actually want.

Prompting Is Iterative — One Draft Is Rarely Enough

The best prompt writers don't get it perfect on the first try — they treat it as a conversation. Here's how to iterate when the first output isn't quite right.

1
Start with your best prompt

Include role, task, context, format, and tone. Give the AI enough to work with, but don't overthink it — a good first attempt will get you 70% of the way there.

2
Identify specifically what's wrong

Don't just say "this isn't right." Pinpoint what needs to change: too formal? Too long? Missing a key point? Wrong structure? The more specific you are, the better the revision.

"This is good but the tone sounds too salesy. Make it sound more like I'm genuinely checking in as a person, not as a salesperson."
3
Give targeted refinement instructions

Ask for specific changes rather than starting over. You can also ask for variations: "Give me three different subject line options for this email."

"Shorten the second paragraph to two sentences and make the call to action more specific — instead of 'let me know if you're interested,' make it 'are you free for a 20-minute call on Thursday?'"
4
Edit the final output yourself

Even after several iterations, AI output needs a human pass. Add your personal touch, adjust anything that doesn't sound like you, and verify any facts before sending.

🎯

The Professional Mindset on Prompting

Strong prompt writers think about AI the same way a good manager thinks about delegating: give clear context, explain the goal, specify what "done" looks like, and know that you'll still need to review the output before it goes out. The time you invest upfront in a clear, detailed prompt saves you far more time in editing on the back end.

📌 Article Summary

What You Just Learned

A strong prompt includes up to five elements: Role (who the AI should act as), Task (what to produce), Context (relevant background), Format (length, structure), and Tone (how it should sound).

Vague prompts produce generic outputs. Specific prompts — with real context, real recipients, and clear goals — produce outputs worth using.

Prompting is iterative — identify specifically what's wrong, give targeted refinement instructions, and always do a human edit pass at the end.

The quality of your prompt reflects the clarity of your thinking — if you can't describe what you want precisely, you haven't thought it through enough yet.
