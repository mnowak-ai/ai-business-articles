Ethics Check — When Should AI NOT Be Used?

Knowing how to use AI effectively is valuable. Knowing when not to use it — or when to use it with serious caution — might be even more important. This is where business judgment meets ethical responsibility.

This certification has covered a lot of ground on what AI can do and how to use it well. But there's one more essential conversation: not every situation calls for AI. And in some situations, using AI — without thinking through the ethical implications — can cause real harm to real people.

This isn't about being anti-AI. It's about being a thoughtful professional who knows that powerful tools require thoughtful judgment. The question "should we use AI for this?" is one of the most important questions in modern business — and it doesn't always have a "yes" answer.

Four Questions to Ask Before Deploying AI

When a business is considering using AI for a task — especially one that affects people's lives — these four questions should always be asked first. Click each one to explore what you're really evaluating.

⚖️
Could an error cause serious harm to someone?
+
The higher the stakes of a wrong decision, the more caution is warranted. An AI that occasionally recommends the wrong movie is one thing. An AI that occasionally misdiagnoses a medical condition, wrongly flags someone as a fraud risk, or incorrectly denies someone housing is entirely different. The potential severity of error should directly determine how much human oversight is required — and whether AI should be used at all.
Ask yourself
If this AI is wrong in the worst-case scenario, what happens to the person on the receiving end?
👁️
Will people know AI is involved in decisions that affect them?
+
Transparency is a core principle of ethical AI. When AI is making or influencing decisions about people — hiring, credit, pricing, content moderation — those people generally have a right to know. Hidden AI decision-making erodes trust, removes the ability to appeal unfair outcomes, and is increasingly being regulated against in many jurisdictions.
Ask yourself
If the people affected by this AI decision found out AI was involved, would they feel that was fair and transparent?
🔍
Could this AI perpetuate or amplify existing bias?
+
As we covered in the bias article, AI trained on historical data can learn and replicate historical inequalities at scale. Before deploying AI in any context where fairness matters — hiring, lending, law enforcement, healthcare — businesses must actively test for bias across different demographic groups. If the training data is skewed, the AI's decisions will be too.
Ask yourself
Have we tested this AI's outputs across different demographic groups? Are the outcomes equally fair?
🧭
Is there a human who can be held accountable for the outcome?
+
"The AI decided" is not an acceptable answer when something goes wrong. In every AI deployment that affects people's lives, there must be a named human — or team — who is responsible and accountable for the outcomes. Without clear human accountability, there's no mechanism for correction when things go wrong, and no one to answer to the people who were harmed.
Ask yourself
If this AI produces a harmful outcome, who in our organization is responsible for fixing it and answering to those affected?

Clear "No" Zones — Where AI Shouldn't Be Used Alone

These are areas where current AI technology, combined with what's at stake, makes fully autonomous AI decision-making ethically indefensible. AI can assist, inform, or suggest — but a human must be the final decision-maker.

⚕️
Final medical diagnoses or treatment decisions

AI can help flag anomalies in imaging or suggest differential diagnoses — but the final clinical judgment must belong to a licensed physician who can weigh the full context of a patient's situation.

⚖️
Criminal sentencing or legal judgments

Predictive risk tools have been used in some justice systems, but their bias problems and lack of transparency make fully automated sentencing deeply problematic. Human judges are required for reasons of fairness and due process.

💔
Mental health crisis support

AI chatbots can provide general wellness support, but someone in a genuine mental health crisis needs a trained human professional — not an automated system that can't read emotional nuance or call for help when needed.

🗳️
Political targeting or voter manipulation

Using AI to generate highly personalized political messaging at scale — especially when using data people didn't knowingly consent to share — crosses a line from communication into manipulation.

🎭
Creating convincing fake content about real people

Deepfakes, AI-generated statements attributed to real individuals, and synthetic media designed to deceive — these applications of AI actively undermine truth and trust in public discourse.

The question isn't just "can we use AI for this?" It's "should we — and if we do, how do we make sure it's fair, transparent, and accountable?"

Real Scenarios — Ethical or Not?

These are real-world AI applications. Each one raises different ethical questions. Think through each before reading the verdict.

✅ Generally Acceptable
AI that suggests Netflix shows to watch

Low stakes, transparent (Netflix is a recommendation platform), no serious harm from wrong suggestions, and users have agency to ignore recommendations. AI is appropriate here.

⚠️ Serious Concerns
AI that makes final hiring decisions with no human review

High stakes (affects someone's livelihood), documented bias risks, no human accountability, and people are affected without knowing AI decided. Human review is non-negotiable here.

🟡 Acceptable With Safeguards
AI that flags loan applications for human review

AI assisting a human who makes the final decision is very different from AI making the final decision alone. The safeguards that matter: bias testing, human override, transparency to applicants, and appeal process.

⚠️ Not Acceptable
AI generating fake news articles attributed to real journalists

Deliberately deceptive, harms both the named journalists and the public's ability to trust information. No business justification outweighs the damage to truth and trust in public discourse.

✅ Generally Acceptable
AI drafting customer support email templates for agents to review and send

Human agent reviews before sending, relatively low stakes, improves efficiency without removing human judgment. This is the model of human-AI collaboration done right.

🟡 Requires Careful Design
AI used in school to flag students at risk of dropping out

Potentially beneficial — if it leads to support. Ethically problematic if it leads to differential treatment or creates self-fulfilling prophecies. The use of the output matters enormously.

The Mindset to Carry Forward

Throughout your career, you'll encounter dozens of decisions about whether and how to use AI. The professionals who get this right are the ones who ask the hard questions early — before the system is built, not after something goes wrong. Ethical AI use isn't a constraint on innovation. It's what makes innovation sustainable and trustworthy.
