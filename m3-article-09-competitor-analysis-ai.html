How to Do a Competitor Analysis Using AI

A competitor analysis isn't just a list of other companies. Done right, it reveals the gaps in the market — the things nobody else is doing well that your product could own. AI makes this process faster and more thorough than ever before.

Remember the Nike/Adidas/Under Armour scenario from the video? That activity — comparing competitors to find positioning advantages — is one of the most common business research tasks in the real world. Every product launch, partnership pitch, and strategic plan involves some version of it.

A competitor analysis answers two essential questions: What are competitors doing well enough that you need to match it? And more importantly: What gaps are they leaving open that you could fill? Those gaps are where competitive advantage lives.

AI can help you build this analysis in a structured, repeatable way. Here's the four-step process.

The 4-Step AI Competitor Analysis Process

01
Map the Competitive Landscape

Start by identifying who all the relevant competitors are — direct competitors (same product, same customer) and indirect competitors (different product, same need).

"Who are the top direct and indirect competitors for [product/service] targeting [customer type]? Group them by competitive tier: major players, mid-tier, and emerging challengers."
02
Build a Feature & Positioning Matrix

Compare competitors across the dimensions that matter most to customers: features, pricing, target customer, key message, and known weaknesses.

"Create a comparison table for [Competitor A, B, C] across: pricing tier, top 3 features, target customer, brand positioning statement, and most common customer complaint."
03
Identify Gaps & Whitespace

This is the most valuable step. Based on the matrix, what customer needs are underserved? What feature combinations don't exist? What customer segments are being ignored?

"Based on the competitor analysis above, identify 3 specific gaps in the market — customer needs or segments that none of the current competitors are fully serving. For each gap, explain why it represents an opportunity."
04
Develop a Positioning Statement

Use the gaps you found to define how a new product could differentiate. A positioning statement tells the market exactly why you're different — and for whom.

"Based on the gap [specific gap identified in Step 3], write a positioning statement for a new product that owns that space. Format: 'For [target customer] who [need], [product] is the [category] that [differentiator] unlike [competitor]'."

The goal of competitor analysis isn't to understand what to copy. It's to find the space between competitors that's underserved — and build something that lives exactly there.

AI Is Excellent at Pattern Recognition — But You Find the Strategy

AI can build a competitor matrix, summarize strengths and weaknesses, and even surface potential gaps — all faster than any human researcher. But the strategic question — "Is this gap worth pursuing? Do we have the capability to fill it? Does it align with our vision?" — is a judgment call only humans can make. AI gives you the landscape. You decide where to build.

📌 Article Summary

What You Just Learned

A competitor analysis isn't about copying what others do — it's about finding the gaps they leave open and building something that lives in that underserved space.

The 4-step AI process: map the landscape, build a feature matrix, identify gaps, develop a positioning statement. Each step builds directly on the last.

The Gap column of a competitor matrix is where competitive strategy is born — it shows where customer needs exist that nobody is fully meeting.

AI maps the landscape — but you decide the strategy. Whether a gap is worth pursuing requires human judgment about capability, timing, and vision that AI can't provide.
