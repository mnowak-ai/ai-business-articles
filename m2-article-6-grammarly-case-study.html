Real Company Spotlight: How Grammarly Uses AI to Improve Business Writing

Grammarly started as a grammar checker. Today it's one of the most widely used AI writing tools in the world — and it offers a clear window into how AI can help people communicate more professionally at work.

We've covered what good business writing looks like. Now let's look at a real company that built a business around helping people achieve it — and how AI powers every part of what they do.

Grammarly is used by over 30 million people daily, including employees at 96% of the Fortune 500. That adoption tells you something important: professional writing is a widespread challenge, and businesses are willing to invest in solving it. Let's walk through the case study.

✍️

About Grammarly

Founded in 2009, Grammarly is an AI-powered writing assistant used by 30+ million daily users across individuals and enterprises. Their business product serves teams ranging from startups to Fortune 500 companies who want consistent, professional written communication. Revenue estimated at over $200 million annually.

The Case Study: Problem → Solution → Result

😓
Problem
Professional Writing Quality Is Inconsistent — and Costly

Companies lose enormous amounts of time and money to poorly written communication. Unclear emails create confusion and require follow-up. Tone mistakes damage client relationships. Grammar errors in proposals undermine credibility. The challenge isn't that people don't know writing matters — it's that in the flow of a busy workday, most people don't have time to carefully edit everything they write. They send emails quickly, dash off reports under deadline, and move on. The cumulative cost of that rushed communication is enormous.

🤖
Solution
Real-Time AI Writing Assistance — Everywhere You Write

Grammarly's solution was to build AI that could operate in real time, inside the tools people already use — Gmail, Google Docs, Outlook, Slack, LinkedIn, and more. Rather than requiring users to go to a separate tool, Grammarly sits quietly in the background and surfaces suggestions as they type. The AI checks not just grammar and spelling but also tone, clarity, formality, conciseness, and engagement — and it explains why each suggestion matters, teaching the user over time.

📈
Result
Measurably Better Communication, Consistently

Enterprise customers using Grammarly Business report significant improvements in writing quality across teams. The key insight from Grammarly's research: writing quality isn't just a nice-to-have — it's a business outcome driver. Teams that communicate more clearly make decisions faster, experience fewer miscommunications, and present more professionally to clients. The AI doesn't replace the human writer — it makes the human writer more consistently excellent.

What Grammarly's AI Actually Checks

Most people think of Grammarly as a spell-checker. It's significantly more than that. Here's what the AI actually analyzes.

📝
Grammar & Spelling

Catches errors most spellcheckers miss — context-dependent mistakes like "your" vs "you're," incorrect verb forms, and punctuation issues.

📌 "Your going to the meeting" → "You're going to the meeting"
🎭
Tone Detection

Analyzes the emotional register of your writing and flags when it might come across as too harsh, too casual, passive-aggressive, or unclear in intent.

📌 Flags "As I mentioned" as potentially passive-aggressive in context
✂️
Conciseness

Identifies wordy phrases and suggests tighter alternatives — cutting filler that adds length without adding meaning.

📌 "Due to the fact that" → "Because"
🎯
Clarity

Flags sentences that are confusing or ambiguous and suggests clearer alternatives. Particularly useful for complex technical or business content.

📌 Flags sentences over 40 words and suggests breaking them up
👔
Formality Level

Detects when your writing is more casual or formal than the context warrants and suggests adjustments to match the audience and situation.

📌 Flags "thx" and "lol" in a professional email context
🤖
Generative Suggestions

The newer Grammarly AI can rewrite entire sentences or paragraphs — not just flag issues, but propose complete alternatives you can accept or modify.

📌 "Improve this paragraph for clarity" → complete rewrite suggestion

Case Study Analysis
Why did Grammarly embed into existing tools (Gmail, Docs, Slack) instead of building a standalone writing platform?
The best productivity tool is one you don't have to think about using. If Grammarly required users to copy-paste their writing into a separate app, most people wouldn't bother — especially when busy. By working inside the tools people already use every day, Grammarly eliminated the friction that would prevent adoption. This is the same insight Shopify had: embed AI where people already work.
Why does Grammarly explain its suggestions rather than just making automatic fixes?
Because the goal isn't just to fix this email — it's to make you a better writer over time. By explaining why a suggestion is made ("this sounds passive-aggressive because..."), Grammarly builds the user's judgment and skills. That makes the product more valuable long-term and creates deeper user loyalty. It's teaching, not just correcting.
What is a potential limitation or risk of relying heavily on Grammarly?
Over-reliance could mean writers stop developing their own judgment about tone and clarity — they just accept whatever Grammarly suggests. The AI also can't know your specific relationship with the reader, the cultural context, or the organizational dynamics that might make a suggestion wrong in your specific situation. It's a tool that needs a thinking human behind it, not a replacement for one.
💡

How to Use Grammarly Strategically

Don't just accept every suggestion blindly. Read each one and understand why it's being made. Use the "Goals" feature to set your audience (expert, general, knowledgeable), formality (informal, neutral, formal), and intent (inform, describe, convince, tell a story) — this makes suggestions far more relevant. And remember: Grammarly is a second pair of eyes, not a replacement for your own judgment about what to say.
