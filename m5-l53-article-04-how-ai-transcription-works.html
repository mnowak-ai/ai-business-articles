Lesson 5.3 · Meeting Notes & Summaries

How AI Transcription Works — and How It Becomes a Summary

When you join a meeting with Otter.ai running, something sophisticated is happening in the background. Understanding the technology — and its limits — is what separates someone who uses AI meeting tools well from someone who trusts them blindly.

From Sound Waves to Structured Notes: The Pipeline

AI transcription isn't one process — it's a four-stage pipeline where each stage transforms the meeting data into something more structured and useful. Here's exactly what happens:

1

Audio Capture & Processing

The AI tool connects to your meeting audio (via mic or video platform integration). Audio is streamed in real time to the AI's servers, where it's cleaned up — background noise reduced, volume normalized — to maximize transcription accuracy.

Limitation: Poor audio quality (low-quality microphones, echo-heavy rooms, people talking over each other) is the #1 cause of transcription errors. Garbage in, garbage out.
2

Speech-to-Text Conversion (ASR)

Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) converts spoken audio to text in near-real-time. Modern ASR models are trained on millions of hours of speech and achieve 85–95% accuracy in clean conditions — but accuracy drops significantly with accents, technical jargon, or overlapping speech.

Limitation: Industry-specific terms, proper nouns, and uncommon names are frequent error points. "Let's use React" might transcribe as "Let's use reacts."
3

Speaker Diarization

The AI identifies who is speaking when — separating the transcript into speaker-attributed lines. This uses voice fingerprinting: the AI analyzes vocal patterns to distinguish Speaker A from Speaker B throughout the meeting.

Limitation: Works best after calibration. Early in a meeting with many participants, speakers may be mislabeled. The AI often asks attendees to say their names aloud to improve accuracy.
4

AI Summarization & Extraction

Once transcribed, an LLM (large language model) analyzes the full transcript to extract structured outputs: a summary of key points, decisions made, and action items with owners. This is where the raw transcript becomes a usable document.

Limitation: The LLM identifies action items from linguistic patterns ("I'll...", "Can you...", "We need to...") but can miss commitments made with unusual phrasing or heavy implication.

What AI Transcription Can and Can't Do

✅ AI Does Well❌ AI Struggles With
Capturing every word in clean audio conditions (85–95% accuracy)Heavy accents, non-native speakers, regional dialects
Attributing statements to speakers once calibratedDistinguishing speakers with similar voices or when multiple people talk at once
Identifying explicit action items ("I'll do X by Friday")Implied commitments, sarcasm, or "we should probably..." statements
Summarizing the general direction of a long discussionCapturing nuanced context — why a decision was made, not just that it was
Technical terms it's been trained onNew jargon, brand names, internal project codenames, people's names
Producing a searchable, text-based record instantlyUnderstanding that a "decision" was actually hypothetical or exploratory

AI produces the first draft of a meeting record in seconds. But a first draft is not a final record. The human review step is not optional — it's where accuracy and accountability are confirmed.

🧠 Quick Check
During a meeting, the CEO says: "Look, we probably should move forward with the acquisition — but let me sleep on it and we'll confirm Tuesday." Otter.ai transcribes this as: "Action Item — CEO: Move forward with acquisition." What has gone wrong?
📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

AI transcription is a 4-stage pipeline: audio capture → speech-to-text → speaker identification → AI summarization and extraction. Each stage has specific limitations.

AI is excellent at converting clean audio to text and identifying explicit action items. It struggles with accents, jargon, implied commitments, and contextual nuance.

The final human review step is not optional. AI produces a first draft — accurately structured but potentially containing errors that only a meeting participant would recognize.
