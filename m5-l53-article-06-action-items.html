Writing Action Items That Actually Get Done

An action item written poorly is a task that won't get done. This isn't a character flaw — it's a structural problem. The good news is there's a simple, learnable formula that eliminates ambiguity and makes every commitment in a meeting trackable and accountable.

Why Most Action Items Fail

Research on follow-through in professional settings consistently shows the same pattern: tasks without clear ownership and deadlines get done at roughly half the rate of tasks with both. This isn't about effort — it's about specificity. The human brain needs clarity to prioritize. Vague commitments get perpetually deprioritized in favor of anything concrete.

The pattern appears in meeting after meeting: "We should follow up on that" turns into nothing. "Someone look into the pricing thing" gets dropped. "Let's figure out the launch date" never results in a launch date being figured out. The problem is structural: these aren't action items. They're vague intentions without owners or deadlines.

If an action item doesn't have one person's name and one specific date on it, it's not an action item. It's a wish.

The Three-Part Formula

Every action item — whether written by a human or extracted by AI — must have exactly three parts:

Part 1
Task
+
Part 2
Owner
+
Part 3
Deadline

Task: A specific verb-driven description of what needs to happen. "Email" not "follow up." "Draft" not "work on." "Confirm" not "check." The task should be specific enough that anyone reading it knows exactly what "done" looks like.

Owner: One name. One person. Not "the team," not "marketing," not "someone." When two people own a task, nobody does. If collaboration is needed, one person is still accountable and others are supporting.

Deadline: A specific date. Not "soon," not "ASAP," not "next week." If you can't name a date, ask in the meeting: "When can you have that done?" Get a date before the meeting ends.

When AI Extracts Action Items: What to Check

AI meeting tools automatically extract action items from transcripts. But AI can only work with what was actually said. Here's what to check before publishing AI-extracted action items:

🔍
Check for vague tasks

AI extracts "follow up on pricing" because those words were spoken. Before publishing, turn it into something specific.

👤
Verify owners were confirmed in the meeting

AI attributes tasks to whoever spoke the commitment. But if Sarah suggested Marcus do something and Marcus didn't explicitly agree, the owner is uncertain.

📅
Add deadlines where AI left them blank

When no deadline was stated in the meeting, AI leaves the field empty. Before publishing, either confirm a deadline or flag it as "deadline TBD — confirm with owner."

⚠️
Flag tentative commitments

"I can probably do that" is not a commitment. Mark uncertain action items as needing confirmation before including them in the official notes.

🧠 Quick Check
At the end of a meeting, the facilitator says: "OK so the team will take care of the marketing materials." You're the note-taker. What do you do?
📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Every action item needs exactly three parts: a specific task, one owner (one name), and a specific deadline. All three are required. Missing any one significantly reduces the chance it gets done.

"The team will handle it" is not an action item. When ownership is shared, accountability disappears. One name per task — every time.

When reviewing AI-extracted action items, check for: vague task descriptions, unconfirmed owners, missing deadlines, and tentative commitments that weren't explicitly agreed to.
