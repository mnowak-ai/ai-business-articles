Subject Lines, Headlines, and Opening Sentences — How to Get Read

The best-written email in the world doesn't matter if nobody opens it. Your subject line and first sentence determine whether your message gets read or ignored.

A senior executive receives 150+ emails per day. They scan subject lines for about half a second each before deciding open, skip, or delete. Your subject line is not a label for your email — it's an advertisement for it. It needs to tell the reader exactly why they should open this one right now.

Most professional writing courses skip this entirely and go straight to body content. But no amount of excellent body content helps if the reader never gets there. This article gives you the practical formulas that consistently get emails opened and messages read.

What Makes a Subject Line Work

A good subject line does one of three things: it signals urgency, it delivers value upfront, or it makes the ask instantly clear. A bad subject line is vague, generic, or tries to be clever at the expense of clarity. Compare these pairs.

❌ Weak
"Following up"
On what? From when? Could be anything. Reader has no reason to prioritize this.
✅ Strong
"Q3 Proposal — Decision Needed by Thursday"
Specific topic, clear deadline, obvious action required. Reader knows exactly what this is and when it matters.
❌ Weak
"Quick question"
Provides zero information about what the question is. Reader can't prioritize this without opening it.
✅ Strong
"Quick question: can you join Thursday's 2pm kickoff?"
The question is right there. Reader can answer without even opening the email — which is the goal.
❌ Weak
"Introduction"
Who? Why? What do they want? Too vague to prioritize. Likely to be skipped or deleted.
✅ Strong
"Introduction: Marcus Webb, referred by Sarah Chen at Acme"
A name, a referral, and a connection — the reader knows who sent this and has context to decide whether to open it.
❌ Weak
"RE: RE: RE: RE: Meeting"
A long reply chain with a generic subject — reader has to guess what's being discussed and where the thread stands.
✅ Strong
"Thursday meeting confirmed — new location attached"
Status is clear (confirmed), there's new information (new location), and the reader knows to open it for an update.

Four Subject Line Formulas That Work

Formula 1
[Topic] — [Action needed] by [Deadline]
"Budget approval — sign-off needed by Friday"
"Contract renewal — decision needed this week"
Formula 2
Quick [ask/question/update]: [Specific thing]
"Quick update: project is on track for October 1st"
"Quick question: who owns the client onboarding doc?"
Formula 3
[Result/Outcome] — [Context]
"Q3 numbers are in — we hit target"
"Client meeting confirmed — Thursday 10am"
Formula 4
[Person/Topic] intro, referred by [Name]
"Intro: Jamie Park, referred by David Chen"
"Connecting: Alex Wu re your fintech article"

Your subject line is the most important sentence in your email. Most people spend 10 seconds on it. They should spend two minutes.

The Opening Sentence — What Comes Next Matters Too

If your subject line is the door, your opening sentence is the handshake. It either reassures the reader they made the right choice opening this — or makes them regret it immediately. Here's what separates strong openers from weak ones.

Getting AI to Write Strong Subject Lines

AI is actually quite good at generating subject line options when prompted well. Try: "Generate 5 subject line options for an email requesting budget approval for a $50,000 software purchase. The decision-maker is a CFO who values brevity. Include: the topic, the ask, and a clear deadline." Then choose the one that best fits your relationship with that specific person. AI gives you options — you pick the right one.

📌 Article Summary

What You Just Learned

Subject lines are advertisements, not labels — they need to tell the reader why to open this email right now, not just what topic it relates to.

Strong subject lines use formulas: Topic + Action + Deadline, Quick [ask/update]: specific content, Result — Context, or Introduction referencing a mutual connection.

The opening sentence should immediately deliver value or state the purpose — not warm up with pleasantries or announce that you're writing to them.

Vague filler openers ("I hope this finds you well," "just touching base") signal poor professional writing habits — replace them with the actual message.
