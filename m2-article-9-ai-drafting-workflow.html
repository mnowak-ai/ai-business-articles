Lesson 2.2 · Using AI to Draft Documents

The AI Drafting Workflow — From Prompt to Published

Having a repeatable workflow for AI-assisted writing is what separates professionals who use AI efficiently from those who waste time on bad drafts. Here's the complete process.

Most people who struggle with AI writing tools don't have a bad tool — they have no workflow. They write a vague prompt, get a mediocre draft, feel frustrated, and either give up or spend an hour trying to fix something that was never going to work. A clear, repeatable process fixes all of that.

The AI drafting workflow has five stages: prepare, prompt, evaluate, refine, and finalize. Each stage has a specific purpose. Skip one and the whole thing breaks down. Follow it and you can produce polished, professional documents in a fraction of the time it would take to write from scratch.

The Five-Stage Workflow

1
Prepare — Know What You Want Before You Start

Before opening any AI tool, answer three questions: Who is this for? What do I want them to think, feel, or do? What are the key facts they need? You cannot write a good prompt without clear answers. If you're fuzzy on these, you'll produce a fuzzy prompt and a useless draft.

Before you write the prompt, know:
📍 The recipient and your relationship with them
🎯 The specific purpose of this document
📋 The key facts, data, or decisions it needs to contain
📐 The format, length, and any structural requirements
🎭 The tone that fits this person and this situation
2
Prompt — Give the AI Everything It Needs

Write a complete prompt using the five elements: Role, Task, Context, Format, and Tone. Include the specific facts you gathered in Step 1. The more context you provide, the less editing you'll need to do later. This is where most of your thinking should happen.

Strong prompt example
"Act as a business writing professional. Write a follow-up email to our client Maya Rodriguez, VP of Operations at Thornton Manufacturing. We met at the Chicago trade show last Thursday. She expressed interest in our inventory software and asked for a one-page overview of pricing. She mentioned she has a budget review on October 15th. Email should be warm but professional, 3 short paragraphs, with a clear next step (call this week)."
3
Evaluate — Read It Like an Editor, Not a Proofreader

When you get the first draft, don't just check for typos. Read it from the perspective of the recipient. Does it achieve the purpose? Is the tone right? Is anything factually wrong or suspiciously specific? Does it sound like something a human would actually send? Most first drafts need significant work — that's expected.

Evaluate against these questions
✅ Does it open with the right tone for this relationship?
✅ Is the core message (purpose, ask, next step) clear?
✅ Are there any specific facts or numbers I need to verify?
✅ Does it sound human, or does it sound like AI wrote it?
✅ Is anything missing that this person needs to know?
✅ Is it the right length for the context?
⚠️ Common first-draft problems: AI often opens with "I hope this email finds you well" (generic), uses overly formal language, buries the call to action, or includes plausible-sounding but unverified details. Flag all of these.
4
Refine — Iterate Until It's Right

Give the AI specific refinement instructions rather than starting over from scratch. Tell it exactly what to change and why. Two or three rounds of targeted refinement almost always produces something usable. If after three rounds it's still not working, you may need to rethink the prompt entirely or write the section yourself.

Effective refinement prompts
"The opening paragraph is too formal. Rewrite it to sound warmer and more personal — like I'm genuinely happy to reconnect, not sending a template."

"The call to action is vague. Replace it with: 'Are you free for a 20-minute call Wednesday or Thursday afternoon?'"

"Remove the second paragraph — it's unnecessary context. The email reads better without it."
After 2–3 rounds of specific refinement, most documents reach 85–90% of final quality.
5
Finalize — Your Human Pass Makes It Yours

Even after excellent AI iterations, the final pass is yours. Add your personal touch — a specific detail only you would know, an authentic observation, a personalization that shows you actually know this person. Verify every fact. Read it aloud to check if it sounds natural. Then send it.

Final checklist before sending
☐ All names and titles are correct
☐ Every specific fact or number has been verified
☐ The tone sounds like me, not like a template
☐ The call to action is specific and actionable
☐ I read it aloud and it sounds natural
☐ I'm comfortable putting my name on this

📝 The Prompt
Act as a professional sales writer. Write a follow-up email to James Chen, Head of Operations at Vertex Logistics. We did a product demo of our route optimization software last Tuesday. He seemed genuinely interested during the call — he asked detailed questions about the API integration. But he hasn't replied to my follow-up email from Thursday. 🤖 AI First Draft — Before Human Review
Subject: Following Up on Our Route Optimization Demo Hi James, I hope this message finds you well. I wanted to follow up on our demonstration of our route optimization software from last Tuesday and see if you had any further thoughts or questions regarding the product and its potential integration capabilities for Vertex Logistics. I understand that you may be busy, and I want to ensure that you have all the information you need to make an informed decision. Please don't hesitate to reach out if you would like to schedule a follow-up call or if there is anything else I can assist you with. Best regards, [Name]
⚠️ "I hope this message finds you well" — generic opener, AI cliché
⚠️ Way too long — asked for 3 sentences, got 3 paragraphs
⚠️ "make an informed decision" — sounds salesy and stiff
⚠️ No specific question or easy next step
🔄 Refinement Instructions Given
"Too long and generic. Cut it to 3 sentences max. Remove 'I hope this message finds you well' — start directly. The question at the end should be: 'Would it be useful to jump on a quick call this week so I can answer any questions about the API integration?' Make it sound like I'm a real person who actually remembers our conversation, not a template."
✅ Final Version — After Human Pass
Subject: Quick Check-In — Vertex + Route Optimization Hi James, Wanted to check back in since we spoke last Tuesday — you had some great questions about the API integration and I want to make sure you got everything you needed. Would it be useful to jump on a 20-minute call this week so I can walk you through those details directly? Either way, happy to send over any additional documentation in the meantime. [Name]
✅ Opens with a specific reference to James — sounds like a real person
✅ Tight — under 60 words total
✅ Specific, easy-to-answer question about the API integration
✅ Warm and human — no corporate filler language

The workflow isn't about making AI do all the work. It's about making sure your time is spent where it adds the most value — thinking, judging, and personalizing — while AI handles the heavy lifting of producing words.

Practice Makes the Workflow Faster

The first time you use this workflow it might feel slow — you're not used to writing prompts or evaluating AI output as an editor. By the tenth time, it becomes second nature. The goal is to internalize the workflow until it's automatic: prepare, prompt, evaluate, refine, finalize. Every time. For everything that matters.

📌 Article Summary

What You Just Learned

The AI drafting workflow has five stages: Prepare (know your goal), Prompt (give the AI everything it needs), Evaluate (read as an editor), Refine (targeted iterations), Finalize (your human pass).

First drafts are starting points, not finished products. Most AI first drafts have 3–5 issues that need addressing before they're ready to send.

Refinement is iterative — give specific instructions, not vague complaints. Two or three rounds of targeted feedback almost always produces something usable.

AI-assisted writing saves significant time — not by eliminating work but by eliminating the blank page, so your time is spent where it matters most.
