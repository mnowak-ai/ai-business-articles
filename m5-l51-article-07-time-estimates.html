Lesson 5.1 · Time Management with AI

Time Estimates and Why We're All Terrible At Them

Even experienced professionals consistently underestimate how long tasks take. This isn't a personal failing — it's a documented cognitive bias. Understanding why it happens is the first step to fixing it — and AI can help.

The Planning Fallacy: A Bias Hardwired In

In 1979, psychologists Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky identified a cognitive bias called the Planning Fallacy: the tendency to predict tasks will take less time than they actually do — even when you have personal experience with similar tasks running over.

This surprises people. Surely if you've done something before and it took longer than expected, you'd correct for that next time? But research shows you don't. Even knowing about the bias, people continue to underestimate. Even experts in their own field. It's not about intelligence or experience — it's about how our brains construct mental simulations of the future.

The Sydney Opera House was estimated to take 4 years and cost $7M. It took 14 years and cost $102M. Your Tuesday task list has the same problem — just smaller stakes.

Why We Underestimate: Three Root Causes

🔭

We plan for the best-case scenario

When imagining a task, our brain pictures it going smoothly — no file that won't open, no colleague who needs three back-and-forths, no system outage. These friction points are predictable but we systematically ignore them when estimating.

🎯

We forget task overhead

We estimate the core work time but forget the surrounding overhead: finding the relevant files, re-reading the context, writing the follow-up after the meeting, transitioning to the next task. Overhead typically adds 20–40% to task time.

⚡

We assume we'll be focused and undisrupted

Our estimate assumes uninterrupted concentration. In reality, the average knowledge worker is interrupted 15+ times per day, and each interruption costs 23 minutes of recovery. Our plans never account for this.

Three Ways to Estimate Better

1.5×
The Multiplier Rule
Take your initial estimate and multiply it by 1.5x–2x. Simple, fast, and research-backed. If you think it'll take 1 hour, block 90 minutes.
📊
Reference Class Forecasting
Ask: "How long did similar tasks actually take in the past?" Plan from historical reality, not optimistic prediction. AI tools can track this over time.
+20%
Explicit Overhead Buffer
Add 15–20% to every major task for prep, transitions, and follow-up. Schedule this explicitly rather than hoping it fits in the gaps.

Where AI Fits In

AI addresses the Planning Fallacy in two ways. First, general-purpose AI can suggest time estimates for task types based on aggregate data — giving you an "outside view" that counters your optimistic inside view. "How long does writing a 500-word executive summary typically take a professional?" is a question AI can answer objectively.

Second, specialized tools like Motion and Reclaim track your actual task completion history and build a personalized model. Over time, they stop using average estimates and start using your estimates — which are calibrated to how you actually work. This is one of the most underrated benefits of consistent AI tool use.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

The Planning Fallacy is a universal cognitive bias: humans consistently underestimate task duration, even when they're experienced and even when they're aware of the bias.

Three root causes: imagining best-case scenarios, ignoring task overhead, and assuming uninterrupted focus that rarely exists in real workdays.

Three fixes: the 1.5× multiplier, reference class forecasting (using historical data), and explicitly scheduling overhead buffers.

AI tools help by providing outside-view estimates and, over time, building personalized models of your actual completion patterns — correcting the bias systematically rather than relying on willpower.
