Lesson 5.4 · Professional Judgment

Ethical Decision-Making in Business

Legal and profitable don't always mean right. The most respected professionals aren't just competent — they're trustworthy. Understanding how to reason through ethical complexity is a skill, and like every skill, it can be developed with the right tools.

Ethics Isn't a Feeling — It's a Process

When people say something "feels wrong," they're often sensing an ethical problem they haven't yet articulated. The goal of ethical reasoning is to take that feeling and make it precise — to identify exactly which values or principles are in tension, what the trade-offs are, and how to make a decision you can genuinely stand behind.

Business ethics isn't about finding the "perfect" answer. Most meaningful ethical situations involve competing goods or unavoidable trade-offs. The goal is to reason through them thoughtfully rather than defaulting to what's convenient, expedient, or what everyone else is doing.

The most dangerous ethical failures in business don't happen when people consciously choose to do wrong. They happen when people fail to notice that an ethical question was present at all.

Three Lenses for Ethical Reasoning

Philosophers and ethicists have developed multiple frameworks for thinking about right and wrong. You don't need to pick one — using all three as lenses on the same situation is more powerful than any single approach:

📊
Consequentialist
"What produces the best outcomes for everyone affected?"
Focuses on results. The right action is the one that produces the greatest good for the greatest number. Useful for analyzing impact — but can justify harmful means if the outcome seems good enough.
📜
Deontological
"What duties or rules apply here, regardless of outcome?"
Focuses on rules and rights. Some things are simply wrong — lying, breaking trust, exploiting people — regardless of the outcome they produce. Useful for protecting against rationalizing bad behavior.
🪞
Virtue Ethics
"What would a person of good character do?"
Focuses on who you're becoming through your decisions. Asks: is this the kind of action a person with integrity, courage, and fairness would take? Useful when rules and outcomes both feel insufficient.

Two Quick Tests to Check Your Ethical Reasoning

When you're unsure whether a decision is ethically sound, these two tests cut through the noise quickly:

📰
The Front Page Test
"Would I be comfortable if this decision were reported on the front page of a major newspaper?"
If the honest answer is no — or even "I'd want to explain it carefully" — that's important information. This test catches decisions where the optics suggest something the reasoning is trying to justify away.
🪟
The Transparency Test
"Would I be comfortable if everyone affected by this decision could see exactly how I made it?"
This is especially relevant in AI contexts: if the process of using AI to make a decision would concern the people affected by it, that concern is worth taking seriously before proceeding.
🧠 Quick Check
A manager uses an AI tool to rank employees for performance reviews. The AI consistently gives lower scores to employees who take parental leave, because their output metrics dip during leave periods. The rankings are technically accurate to the data. What is the ethical problem?
📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Ethical reasoning is a process, not a feeling. Three lenses: Consequentialist (best outcomes), Deontological (rules and duties), Virtue ethics (character). Using all three is more powerful than any one alone.

AI can't perform ethical reasoning — it applies patterns, not principles. When AI tools produce outputs with ethical implications (hiring, performance, targeting), humans must apply ethical judgment before acting.

Two quick tests: Front Page ("would I want this reported?") and Transparency ("would I be comfortable if affected people saw my process?"). Both help surface ethical concerns that rationalization tends to suppress.
