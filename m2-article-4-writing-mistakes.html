The 7 Most Common Business Writing Mistakes — And How to Fix Them

Most business writing errors are completely predictable — and completely fixable once you know what to look for. This is your self-audit guide.

After reading thousands of pieces of professional writing, patterns emerge. The same mistakes show up over and over — across industries, across experience levels, across companies. The good news: they're not random. Once you learn to recognize them, you can eliminate them from your own writing in a matter of weeks.

Click each mistake to see what it looks like and how to fix it.

1
Writing Too Informally for the Context
+

Casual language, slang, abbreviations, and emojis have their place — in texts with friends, in informal Slack channels. But when they show up in professional emails, proposals, or reports, they signal that the writer isn't taking the situation seriously. This is the most common mistake among newer professionals who are used to communicating casually online.

❌ Too Informal
"Hey!! Just checking in on that thing we talked about lol — any updates?? Thx 😊"
✅ Professional
"Hi James, following up on our conversation from Tuesday — do you have an update on the timeline? I'd like to confirm before Friday's meeting."
Quick fix: Before sending, ask yourself: would I say this in a job interview? If not, rewrite it.
2
Long, Tangled Sentences That Lose the Reader
+

Many people think long, complex sentences sound more intelligent or thorough. They don't. They make the reader work harder — and busy readers give up. A good rule: if you need to re-read a sentence to understand it, it needs to be broken up or simplified.

❌ Too Long
"Due to the fact that the project, which has been ongoing since the beginning of the third quarter and involves multiple stakeholders across different departments, is now approaching a critical milestone that requires input from all relevant parties before any final decisions can be made, it would be beneficial to schedule a meeting."
✅ Clear
"The project is reaching a key milestone. Before we finalize decisions, we need input from all departments. Let's schedule a meeting this week."
Quick fix: If a sentence is more than 25 words, find the natural break point and split it into two.
3
Missing the Point — No Clear Ask or Next Step
+

A message that doesn't tell the reader what to do next forces them to guess — or reply asking what you actually want. This wastes everyone's time and makes you look disorganized. Every business communication should make it crystal clear: what does the reader need to do, and by when?

❌ No Clear Ask
"Hi team, just wanted to share the updated budget numbers for this quarter. The report is attached. We've made some adjustments based on last month's review. Let me know what you think."
✅ Clear Ask
"Hi team — attached is the updated Q3 budget report. Please review by Friday and reply with any questions or concerns. I'll incorporate feedback before Monday's leadership meeting."
Quick fix: End every email with a single, specific action item: "Please reply by [date]" / "Could you confirm receipt?" / "Let me know if you have questions."
4
Reply-All Chaos and CC Overload
+

Nothing derails inbox productivity faster than unnecessary emails. Copying everyone on every communication, replying-all when only one person needs the response, or CCing managers to appear busy are behaviors that signal poor professional judgment. Your CC list is a decision, not a habit.

❌ CC Overload
Email to 12 people asking a logistical question that only one person can answer, with the entire leadership team cc'd "for visibility."
✅ Targeted
Email directly to the person who can answer. If leadership needs to be aware of the outcome, follow up with a brief update — not a full thread.
Quick fix: Ask for every person in To/CC: "Does this person need to take action or truly need to see this?" If neither, remove them.
5
Jargon and Buzzwords That Sound Empty
+

Every industry has its own language — and some of it is genuinely useful shorthand. But corporate buzzwords often substitute for actual thinking. When writing is full of "leverage our synergies," "move the needle," "circle back," and "low-hanging fruit," it signals that the writer hasn't thought clearly about what they're actually trying to say.

❌ Buzzword Heavy
"We need to leverage our core competencies to move the needle on customer engagement and drive synergistic outcomes across all touchpoints going forward."
✅ Plain and Clear
"We need to improve how we engage customers — specifically by responding faster and personalizing our outreach."
Quick fix: After drafting, highlight any word or phrase that you couldn't easily explain to a high school student. Replace it with plain English.
6
Sending Without Proofreading
+

Typos, grammatical errors, wrong names, and missing attachments all signal carelessness — regardless of how smart the content is. In a professional context, errors undermine your credibility. This is especially true in high-stakes communications like proposals, client emails, and executive memos. The five seconds it takes to re-read is always worth it.

❌ Not Proofread
"Dear Mr Smith, Thank you for you're time yesterday. As discussed, I've attached the Proposal for you're review. Let me know if their are any questions. Best, [Name"
✅ Proofread
"Dear Mr. Smith, Thank you for your time yesterday. As discussed, I've attached the proposal for your review. Please let me know if you have any questions. Best regards, [Name]"
Quick fix: Read your email out loud before sending. Your ear catches mistakes your eyes skip over. Also: always check that mentioned attachments are actually attached.
7
Burying the Most Important Point
+

Many writers tell a story that builds to a conclusion — they explain the background, describe the situation, list the considerations, and finally get to the point. This works for novels. It doesn't work for business writing, where readers often skim, read the first two sentences, and move on. Put the most important thing first, every time.

❌ Point Buried
"As you know, we've been tracking the performance of our social media campaigns throughout Q3. Based on our analysis, looking at metrics including engagement, reach, and conversion data, and comparing them against last quarter... [3 more paragraphs]... so we recommend pausing the Instagram spend."
✅ Point First
"Recommendation: Pause Instagram ad spend for Q4. Our Q3 analysis shows engagement down 34% and cost per conversion up 60% vs. last quarter. Supporting data attached."
Quick fix: Practice the "newspaper test" — write your emails like newspaper articles. Headline first (what happened), then details. Readers can stop at any point and still have the key information.

🤖

AI Makes Some of These Better — and Some Worse

AI tools like Grammarly and ChatGPT can catch grammar errors and improve sentence length (mistakes 2 and 6). But they can actually make mistakes 1, 5, and 7 worse — AI often produces overly enthusiastic casual language or buries recommendations in lengthy preamble. Know which mistakes AI helps with and which ones require your own editorial judgment.

📌 Article Summary

What You Just Learned

The 7 most common business writing mistakes: too informal, long tangled sentences, missing the ask, CC overload, empty jargon, not proofreading, and burying the key point.

Put the most important information first — business readers skim and often don't read to the end.

Reading out loud before sending catches errors your eyes miss. Always verify attachments are actually attached.

AI can help with some mistakes (grammar, sentence length) but can make others worse (tone, burying the point) — use your own judgment.
