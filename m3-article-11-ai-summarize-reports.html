Lesson 3.2 · Using AI for Research

How to Ask AI to Summarize Research Reports

You have a 60-page Gartner report and 20 minutes before a meeting. AI can turn that into an actionable brief — but only if you ask it the right way. The difference between a useful summary and a useless one is entirely in how you frame the request.

The Gartner slide in the lesson video made a clear point: AI accelerates research by rapidly summarizing large amounts of market and competitor information. This is one of the highest-value AI use cases in business — and one of the most commonly done poorly.

The problem most people run into is asking AI to "summarize this" without any further instruction. That produces a generic, comprehensive summary of everything — which is usually not what you need. A useful research summary is targeted, structured, and calibrated to a specific audience and decision. The prompts below will get you there every time.

The Executive Brief
Use when: A busy manager or executive needs the essential story in under 2 minutes.
📋 Prompt
"I'm going to paste a research report. Summarize it as a 1-page executive brief for a [VP of Marketing / CEO / board member]. Include only: the single most important finding, what it means for our business, and one recommended action. Maximum 200 words. Do not include background context — assume the reader knows the industry."
💡 The "do not include background context" instruction is critical — it prevents AI from wasting your executive's time with industry definitions they already know.
The Key Findings Extractor
Use when: You need to pull the specific data points and statistics out of a long report quickly.
📋 Prompt
"Extract exactly 5 key findings from this research report. For each finding: state it in one sentence, include the specific number or statistic that supports it, and note the page or section it came from. Format as a numbered list. Do not include analysis or interpretation — just the facts as stated in the document."
💡 Separating findings from interpretation is important — it lets you verify the raw facts independently before deciding what they mean for your business.
The Decision-Focused Summary
Use when: You're using this report to make a specific business decision and need the report to speak to that question.
📋 Prompt
"Summarize this report specifically through the lens of this question: [Should we expand into the Southeast Asian market in 2025?]. Pull only the sections, data points, and findings that are directly relevant to answering this question. Ignore everything else. End with a one-sentence summary of what the report's data suggests about this decision."
💡 This prompt filters the entire report down to only what's relevant to your specific decision — saving you from reading sections that have nothing to do with your question.
The Competitor Intelligence Summary
Use when: You're summarizing a competitor's annual report, press release, or industry analysis that mentions competitors.
📋 Prompt
"From this document, extract all information about [Competitor Name]. Organize it into four categories: (1) what they say their strengths are, (2) what problems or challenges they acknowledge, (3) what their stated priorities or plans are for the next 12 months, and (4) any numbers (revenue, market share, growth rates) mentioned. Flag anything that seems like marketing spin rather than factual disclosure."
💡 The "flag marketing spin" instruction helps distinguish between factual disclosures (which you can rely on) and aspirational claims (which you should verify independently).
The Risk Extractor
Use when: You need to identify all the risk factors, warnings, or caveats mentioned in a research report before making a recommendation.
📋 Prompt
"Read this report and extract every risk, uncertainty, caveat, or 'however' statement mentioned by the authors. Group them into: (1) market risks, (2) competitive risks, (3) execution risks, and (4) data limitations or methodological caveats. This is for a risk assessment — include everything, even if it seems minor."
💡 Reports often bury risk disclosures in footnotes and appendices. This prompt makes sure you find them all — which protects you from presenting overconfident conclusions to leadership.

The value of AI summarization isn't just speed — it's focus. A well-prompted AI summary extracts exactly what you need from a document while filtering out everything you don't. That's a skill multiplier for anyone who works with information.

⚠️

Never Present an AI Summary Without Spot-Checking It

AI can misread numbers, confuse similar company names, or occasionally attribute a finding to the wrong section of a report. Before sharing any AI-generated summary with a manager, client, or team, spot-check the 2–3 most important facts against the original document. This takes 3–5 minutes and is the difference between being reliable and being embarrassed. Verify once, present with confidence.

📌 Article Summary

What You Just Learned

"Summarize this" is one of the weakest research prompts you can write. Always specify: who is the audience, what specific information do you need, how long should the output be, and what should be excluded.

The five summarization modes — executive brief, key findings, decision focus, competitor intel, and risk extraction — are each optimized for a different use case. Matching the right prompt to the right situation is the skill.

Always spot-check the 2–3 most important facts in any AI summary before presenting it. This takes 5 minutes and protects your credibility completely.

The best summarization prompts tell AI what to exclude, not just what to include — filtering out methodology, background context, or irrelevant sections makes the output dramatically more useful.
