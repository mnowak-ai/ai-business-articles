Lesson 3.3 · Evaluating AI Outputs

What to Do When AI Gives You Conflicting Information

Ask the same question twice — or to two different tools — and you may get two different answers. This isn't always a sign that AI is broken. Often it reveals something more interesting about the data itself.

It happens more often than most people expect: you ask AI a research question, get a specific answer, ask again — and get a different number. Or you run the same query through two tools and they disagree. Your first instinct might be to assume one of them is hallucinating. But conflicting AI responses are often a signal worth investigating, not just an error to dismiss.

Understanding why AI gives conflicting answers — and having a systematic process for resolving the conflict — turns what feels like a frustrating problem into a genuine research skill.

Why AI Responses Conflict: The 4 Root Causes

Before you can resolve a conflict, you need to understand where it comes from. Most AI conflicts trace back to one of four causes.

📏
Different Definitions / Scope
Most common cause — both answers are technically correct, just measuring different things
Response A
"The global e-commerce market is worth $5.8 trillion"
Response B
"Global e-commerce reached $6.3 trillion"
✅ Resolution: Find each figure's original source. One may include China's domestic market; the other may exclude it. One may include digital services; the other only counts retail transactions. Neither is wrong — they measure different scopes. Pick the definition that matches what you actually need to show.
📅
Different Time Periods
One answer reflects an older snapshot; another reflects a more recent update
Response A
"Tesla holds 18% of the global EV market"
Response B
"Tesla's EV market share is approximately 14%"
✅ Resolution: Check the date each figure comes from. EV market share shifts quarterly. The lower figure may reflect more recent data after BYD's growth. Always verify which time period applies and cite the date alongside any market share figure.
🔤
Ambiguous Phrasing in Your Question
Your question had more than one valid interpretation, so AI answered different versions
Response A
"Spotify was founded in 2006"
Response B
"Spotify launched in 2008"
✅ Resolution: Both are correct — "founded" (incorporated) vs. "launched" (public availability) are different events. When you asked "when was Spotify founded?", both interpretations were valid. Clarify which milestone you need, then verify that specific date from a primary source.
❌
Genuine Error in One Response
One answer is simply wrong — a true hallucination or outdated information presented as current
Response A
"Slack was acquired by Salesforce in 2021 for $27.7 billion"
Response B
"Slack was acquired by Microsoft in 2021 for $27.7 billion"
✅ Resolution: This is a genuine error — Salesforce is correct, Microsoft is wrong. The dollar figure is the same; the acquirer is different. Cross-reference against a primary source (company press release, SEC filing, or reputable news coverage) to determine which is accurate. This is where independent verification is non-negotiable.

When two AI responses conflict, your first question shouldn't be "which one is right?" It should be "why are they different?" — because the answer often tells you something important about the data itself.

The 4-Step Conflict Resolution Framework

01
Don't Pick Arbitrarily

Never resolve a conflict by choosing the response that seems better, higher, lower, or more recent without evidence. That's just a different kind of random selection.

02
Identify the Root Cause

Ask: is this a definition difference, a time period difference, an ambiguous question, or a genuine error? The resolution approach depends entirely on which it is.

03
Find the Original Source

For each conflicting figure, trace it back to its origin. What report, database, or filing did this number come from? Understanding the source usually resolves the conflict.

04
Choose Based on Fit

Use the figure whose definition, methodology, and time period best matches what your specific research question actually needs to demonstrate.

💡

Conflicting Responses Are Actually a Gift

When AI gives you two different answers, that conflict is forcing you to do the one thing that transforms research from "good enough" to "genuinely reliable" — find the original source. A researcher who encounters conflicting figures and traces both back to their methodological origins comes out of the process with a much deeper understanding of the data than someone who got a single answer and moved on. The conflict does the work of pointing you toward the real research.

📌 What You Just Learned

Article Summary

Conflicting AI responses come from four causes: different definitions/scope, different time periods, ambiguous questions, or a genuine error in one response. Identifying the root cause determines the right resolution.

Never resolve a conflict by picking the higher, lower, more recent, or more impressive figure. Always trace both back to their original sources to understand what each is actually measuring.

Many apparent conflicts aren't errors at all — they're definitional differences. Two figures can both be accurate while measuring different things, different time periods, or different scopes of the same topic.

Conflicting responses are valuable — they force you to find original sources, which always produces better research than accepting a single AI answer without investigation.
