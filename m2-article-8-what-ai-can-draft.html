Lesson 2.2 · Using AI to Draft Documents

What AI Can Help You Draft — And What You Still Need to Own

AI can dramatically speed up your writing process. But it can't replace every part of it — and knowing exactly where the line is separates smart AI users from people who get burned.

There's a tempting shortcut in using AI for writing: just paste in the request, copy the output, and send it. Some people do this and get away with it for a while. But eventually — in a client email, a proposal, a sensitive situation — it becomes obvious that a person wasn't really behind it. The AI missed the context, got the tone wrong, or said something inaccurate.

AI is an extraordinary drafting partner. It's not a replacement for professional judgment, subject matter expertise, or relationship awareness. Understanding exactly which tasks to hand off and which to keep is the foundation of working with AI responsibly and effectively.

The Core Division of Labor

Think of it this way: AI handles the heavy lifting of producing words on a page. You handle everything that requires knowing the context, the relationships, the strategy, and the stakes.

🤖 AI Does Well
⚡
Generating a first draft quickly from your prompt and context
🔄
Producing multiple variations of the same message
✂️
Shortening or expanding content on request
🌐
Adapting tone or style (more formal, friendlier, simpler)
📋
Structuring information into a standard format
🔍
Checking grammar, spelling, and basic clarity
📝
Summarizing long documents you provide
🧠 You Must Own
🎯
Deciding what the document's goal actually is
🤝
Knowing the reader — their context, history, and expectations
✅
Verifying every specific fact, number, and name
⚖️
Judging whether the tone is right for this specific relationship
🧭
Making decisions with ethical or strategic implications
✍️
Adding your personal voice and authentic perspective
🔒
Final approval and responsibility for what gets sent

Task by Task — What AI Can and Can't Do

Let's go deeper on six common writing tasks. For each one, click to see what AI handles well, what you still need to bring, and where the danger zones are.

📧
Professional Emails
+
🤖 AI handles well
Drafting structure, tone calibration, subject line options, standard follow-up templates, grammar and spelling, length adjustments.
🧠 You must bring
The specific history with this person, knowing whether a formal or casual opener fits the relationship, any sensitive context the AI doesn't know about.
⚠️ Watch for: AI doesn't know your relationship history. A prompt like "write a follow-up to a client" won't account for the fact that this particular client has been frustrated recently — you need to flag that context or the AI may produce a tone that makes things worse.
📋
Memos and Announcements
+
🤖 AI handles well
Standard memo format, clear organizational structure, plain-language summaries of policy details you provide, appropriate formal tone.
🧠 You must bring
The actual policy details (AI will invent them if you don't provide them), the organizational context, any legal review needed, understanding of how employees will receive this news.
⚠️ Watch for: If you don't give AI the exact policy details, it will fill in plausible-sounding but fabricated specifics. Always verify that every number, date, and policy detail in the draft matches the actual decision.
📊
Report Sections
+
🤖 AI handles well
Structuring sections, writing smooth transitions, formatting data you provide into clear sentences, generating an outline for a complex report.
🧠 You must bring
The actual data and findings, the interpretation of what the data means, the strategic recommendation, and verification of every figure cited.
⚠️ Watch for: AI will confidently write analysis and recommendations even when it doesn't have enough data to do so accurately. If your prompt is vague, it will invent plausible-sounding conclusions. Give it your real data and ask it to structure and articulate — not to analyze what it doesn't have.
🤝
Client-Facing Proposals
+
🤖 AI handles well
Proposal structure, persuasive framing of benefits, professional language, executive summary drafting, standard sections like scope of work and timeline.
🧠 You must bring
Accurate pricing, actual deliverables and timelines, specific knowledge of the client's needs, your company's real differentiators, legal review of terms.
⚠️ Watch for: Proposals contain commitments — pricing, scope, timelines. AI will fill these in with plausible numbers if you don't specify them. Sending a proposal with AI-generated figures you haven't verified could commit your company to terms you never approved.
💬
Difficult or Sensitive Messages
+
🤖 AI handles well
Drafting a starting point, suggesting diplomatic language, removing accidental aggression from a frustrated first draft, offering alternative phrasings.
🧠 You must bring
The full emotional and relational context, judgment about what the other person needs to hear, whether to send this at all, and the courage to own the message.
⚠️ Watch for: Sensitive messages — performance feedback, apologies, conflict resolution — require genuine human understanding. AI can give you a polished starting point, but sending an AI draft without meaningful personal editing risks coming across as cold or missing the point entirely.
📱
Social Media and Marketing Copy
+
🤖 AI handles well
Generating multiple post variations, adapting content for different platforms, brainstorming hooks and angles, maintaining consistent brand voice if you define it.
🧠 You must bring
Brand voice guidelines, factual accuracy of any claims, cultural awareness and sensitivity review, judgment about what fits your specific audience right now.
⚠️ Watch for: AI doesn't know current events or cultural context. A post that seems fine in isolation might be tone-deaf given something happening in the news. Always review marketing copy against the current moment before publishing.

The test of whether AI is helping you is simple: are you producing better work, faster? If AI is producing work that you're just signing your name to without understanding it — that's not help. That's a liability.

The Ownership Spectrum

Not every writing task is equally high-stakes. Think about AI involvement on a spectrum — the higher the stakes, the more you need to own the output.

The AI Ownership Spectrum
🤖 AI does most of it🧠 You do most of it
📅
Meeting Reminder
Low stakes — AI can handle almost all of it
📢
Team Announcement
Medium stakes — AI drafts, you verify all details
🤝
Client Proposal
High stakes — you own all commitments, AI just helps with structure
⚖️

The Golden Rule of AI-Assisted Writing

Whatever you send or publish with your name on it — or your company's name on it — you are responsible for. AI didn't write it. You did, with AI's help. That means every claim needs to be accurate, every commitment needs to be real, and every message needs to reflect what you actually want to communicate. AI speeds up production. It doesn't transfer responsibility.

📌 Article Summary

What You Just Learned

AI handles drafting, formatting, tone adjustments, and grammar well. You must own the strategy, relationship context, fact-checking, and final responsibility.

For each task type — emails, memos, reports, proposals — AI has specific strengths and specific danger zones. Know both before you rely on it.

Scale your oversight to the stakes: low-stakes writing can lean heavily on AI, high-stakes writing needs much more human involvement and verification.

Whatever goes out with your name on it is your responsibility — AI speeds up production, it doesn't transfer accountability.
