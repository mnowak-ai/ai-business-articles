Lesson 5.4 · Professional Judgment

Bias in AI Recommendations

AI systems learn from human-generated data — which means they learn human biases too. Understanding where AI bias comes from, what it looks like in practice, and how to catch it before it causes harm is one of the most important professional skills in an AI-augmented workplace.

Where AI Bias Comes From

AI bias isn't random — it has predictable sources. AI learns patterns from historical data, which reflects the world as it was — including its inequities, its underrepresented groups, and its historical decisions (many of which were themselves biased). When AI is trained on this data, it learns to replicate those patterns. This isn't a programming error. It's a structural property of how machine learning works.

The second major source is objective function: what the AI was told to optimize for. An AI optimizing for "highest sales conversion" might learn to target people in financial vulnerability — not because it's malicious, but because the data showed that's effective. The optimization metric was never checked against ethical constraints. This is a human design failure, not an AI one — but it's a human's job to catch it.

AI doesn't create bias. It amplifies and systematizes the bias that was already in the world — at a scale that makes it impossible to ignore.

What to Remember

AI bias comes from two primary sources: historical training data that reflects past inequities, and optimization objectives that weren't checked against ethical constraints. Both are human design decisions.

Five bias types to recognize: training data bias, automation bias, confirmation bias, selection bias, and metric bias. Each appears differently in practice — knowing the type helps you address the root cause.

Humans are responsible for AI outputs they act on. "The AI decided it" is not a defense. Professionals who deploy or use AI recommendations have a responsibility to audit for bias — especially when decisions affect people's opportunities, finances, or rights.
