Lesson 3.3 · Evaluating AI Outputs

Is This Source Trustworthy? A Framework for Evaluating AI Research

Not all sources AI points you toward are equally reliable. Knowing how to quickly evaluate source quality — before you stake your work on it — is one of the most transferable research skills you can develop.

When AI provides a statistic or summarizes a study, it often cites a source — or points toward one. But the source itself needs to be evaluated. A citation from a company's own marketing white paper is fundamentally different from a peer-reviewed academic study, even if both look like "research" on the surface.

Source evaluation is the skill of quickly assessing whether information comes from a trustworthy origin. It's been taught in journalism and academia for decades — and it matters more than ever in the age of AI, where confident language makes all sources sound equally authoritative.

The SIFT Framework

SIFT is a practical four-step method developed at Stanford for evaluating sources quickly. Each letter is an action. Use this whenever AI points you to a source or makes a claim you want to verify.

S
Stop
Before you read and react, pause.

Your first impression of a source can activate confirmation bias — if the headline matches what you want to believe, you'll be tempted to accept it without checking. Stop and take a breath before engaging with any source AI cites.

→ Ask: Am I about to accept this because it confirms what I already think?
I
Investigate the Source
Who created this, and why?

Before reading the content, quickly check the source itself. Is it a company with a financial stake in the findings? A think tank with a known ideology? A researcher with credentials in this area? The publisher's identity shapes everything.

→ Ask: Who made this, and do they have a reason to be biased?
F
Find Better Coverage
Does another credible source confirm this?

Don't just evaluate one source in isolation. Search for how other credible outlets have covered the same claim. If multiple independent sources confirm a finding, your confidence can increase significantly. If no one else has covered it, that's a red flag.

→ Ask: What do other reliable sources say about this same claim?
T
Trace Claims to Their Origin
Where did this actually come from?

Many sources cite other sources. Follow the chain back to the original research or data. Often you'll find a statistic that's been misquoted, taken out of context, or the original methodology doesn't support the conclusion being drawn from it.

→ Ask: What is the original source of this data, and does it actually support this claim?

The Source Trust Hierarchy

Not all sources deserve equal credibility. Here's how to think about the relative trustworthiness of different source types — from most to least reliable for business research.

1
Primary Sources — Original Data
Company SEC filings, government databases, peer-reviewed studies, official earnings reports
2
Reputable Independent Research
McKinsey, Gartner, Pew Research, academic journals, major investigative journalism outlets
3
Credible News Coverage with Citations
Reuters, AP, Bloomberg, FT — factual reporting that cites original sources
4
Industry Publications & Blogs (with caution)
Trade journals, industry newsletters — useful context, but check funding sources and methodology
5
Company-Funded Research & Marketing Content
White papers, case studies, surveys commissioned by companies about their own products

Tier 5 isn't always wrong — but it has a systematic incentive problem. A study a company commissions to show their product works should be treated with the same skepticism you'd apply to a customer review written by the company's own employees.

Evaluating a source takes 60 seconds. Presenting a flawed source to leadership takes much longer to recover from. The math is obvious.

AI Often Cites Tier 4–5 Sources Without Warning

When AI summarizes research, it doesn't automatically flag that a statistic came from a company's own marketing white paper rather than independent research. The language sounds equally authoritative regardless of source quality. This is why source evaluation can't be delegated to AI — it requires your judgment to check who created the original data and what their incentives were. Always ask: "Who funded this research, and what did they need it to show?"

📌 What You Just Learned

Article Summary

The SIFT framework: Stop before reacting, Investigate the source, Find better coverage elsewhere, Trace claims to their original origin. Four actions that take under 60 seconds.

Sources exist on a trust hierarchy: primary data and independent research at the top; company-funded white papers at the bottom. Same confident language, very different reliability.

The key source evaluation question is always: who funded this, and what did they need it to show? Financial incentives create predictable bias in research findings.

AI doesn't flag source quality — it presents all citations with equal confidence. Source evaluation is a human judgment that cannot be delegated to the tool that cited the source.
