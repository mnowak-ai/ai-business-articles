📘 Key Term — AI Ethics in Business: AI ethics in business means making sure that when a company uses AI to make decisions — about customers, employees, or money — those decisions are fair, honest, and don't harm people. It also means protecting private information and being honest about how AI is being used.
Scenario 1 of 6⬤ Easier

The AI That Knows Your Shopping Habits

📘 Key Term — Consumer Privacy: Consumer privacy means that customers have the right to control what personal information a company collects about them and how it's used. When a business uses AI to collect or analyze customer data, customers should know about it — and agree to it — before it happens.

Apex Solutions runs an online store. The marketing team wants to use a new AI tool that tracks every customer's browsing behavior and uses that data to automatically send personalized text messages and emails.

The marketing manager says: "We already have a clause that says we may use data to improve the customer experience. That covers it."

You review the current privacy policy. The clause is vague — it mentions "improving the experience" but says nothing about tracking browsing behavior or using it for AI-generated messaging. What do you recommend?

Scenario 2 of 6⬤ Medium

The AI That Charges Different Prices

📘 Key Term — Dynamic Pricing: Dynamic pricing is when a business uses data to charge different customers different prices for the same product or service. When AI uses personal data — like someone's neighborhood or browsing history — to decide how much to charge them, it can become unfair or discriminatory.

Apex Solutions is testing an AI pricing tool for its online services. The tool adjusts the price each customer sees based on their location, device type, and estimated income based on zip code.

During a review, you find the following pattern:

Rural, lower-income zip code, Android$79/month$12

The AI's logic: customers in lower-income areas are "less likely to comparison shop." Customers in rural, lower-income areas are being charged up to $40 more per month for the exact same service. What do you do?

Scenario 3 of 6⬤ Medium

The AI Loan Advisor

📘 Key Term — Algorithmic Fairness: Algorithmic fairness means an AI tool should make decisions that treat people equally — regardless of their age, gender, neighborhood, or background — when those factors have nothing to do with the actual decision. In lending, fairness means loan decisions should be based on financial ability to repay, not on who you are or where you live.

Apex Solutions has a financial services division that uses an AI tool to recommend whether small business loan applicants should be approved or denied. The AI was trained on five years of past loan decisions made by human loan officers at the company.

During a routine audit, you discover a troubling pattern in the AI's decisions over the past year:

Applicant GroupApproval RateDefault Rate
Applicants from high-income zip codes71%8%
Applicants from low-income zip codes34%7%
Applicants under age 3529%6%
Applicants over age 5068%9%

Applicants from low-income zip codes and younger applicants are being denied at much higher rates — even though their actual default rates are lower than the groups being approved. The AI is denying people who are statistically more likely to repay their loans. What do you do?

Scenario 4 of 6⬤ Harder

The Employee Monitoring AI

📘 Key Term — Workplace Privacy: Workplace privacy means employees have some right to privacy even while working — especially regarding their health, personal conversations, and activities outside of work. Using AI to secretly monitor employees' personal behavior crosses an ethical and often legal line.

Apex Solutions has deployed an AI productivity monitoring tool that tracks keystrokes, measures time spent on each application, and flags anyone who drops below a "productivity score" of 70.

You receive a complaint from an employee named Marcus. He was called into a meeting because his score dropped — his manager confronted him with data showing he was taking longer bathroom breaks and spending time in a wellness app during work hours.

You investigate further and discover the AI is also flagging employees who visit union information websites — it categorizes these visits as "non-work activity." Three employees who were flagged for this were later denied promotions. What do you do with these findings?

Scenario 5 of 6⬤ Harder

The AI That Decides Who Gets Help First

📘 Key Term — Customer Equity: Customer equity means treating all of your customers fairly — giving everyone a reasonable level of service regardless of how much money they spend. When AI tools prioritize some customers over others based on their predicted value, people who spend less money may receive worse service even when they have urgent problems.

Apex Solutions runs a customer service center. The new AI triage tool automatically scores each incoming customer request and decides how quickly it gets routed to a human agent. Higher-value customers get connected within 2 minutes. Lower-value customers wait in an automated queue that averages 47 minutes.

You review recent cases and find this example:

"A single mother reported a fraudulent charge on her account. Her case sat in the automated queue for 51 minutes while the charge continued to process. A higher-value customer with a similar fraud report was connected to an agent in 90 seconds."

The COO says: "Premium customers drive 80% of our revenue. It makes business sense to prioritize them." What do you recommend?

Scenario 6 of 6 — Final Challenge⬤ Hardest

📘 Key Term — Regulatory Compliance: Regulatory compliance means following the laws and rules that apply to your industry. In business AI, this includes fair lending laws, consumer protection laws, and data privacy regulations. When a company breaks these rules — even accidentally through an AI tool — the legal and financial consequences can be severe.
