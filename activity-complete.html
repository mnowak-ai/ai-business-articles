Understand the Decision

Before you ask AI anything, you need to understand the problem yourself. That's what makes your prompts — and your thinking — much sharper.

⏱ 5–7 minutes
1

Read the Comparison Document with a Purpose

~3–4 min

Your instructor will give you a document called "Product Comparison: Microsoft Copilot vs Google Gemini for Enterprise Use." As you read, organize your thinking into 3 buckets:

✅ Clear Advantages

  • What does each platform do really well?
  • Where is one clearly better than the other?
  • Any standout features?

⚠️ Concerns or Risks

  • What could go wrong with each option?
  • Are there security issues?
  • Any limitations or weaknesses?

🔁 It Depends Factors

  • Where the best answer changes based on company needs
  • Things that matter more for some businesses
  • Context-specific considerations

Quick reference — the two platforms you're comparing:

🔵 Microsoft Copilot

  • Built into Microsoft 365 (Word, Excel, Teams, Outlook)
  • Strong security certifications
  • Better for data-heavy and finance tasks
  • Preferred by IT teams

🟢 Google Gemini

  • Built into Google Workspace (Gmail, Docs, Slides)
  • Strong for creative and marketing tasks
  • Excellent web search integration
  • Preferred by marketing teams

Evaluate Before You Trust It

⚠️ Watch Out For These Common AI Problems

SECTION 03 OF 06

Prompt Refinement

A better prompt = a better answer. Rewrite your original prompt to force the AI to give you something actually useful.

⏱ 10–12 minutes
1

Upgrade Your Prompt

~5–6 min

A strong business prompt does three things:

🎯 Forces a clear recommendation
🏢 Includes specific business context
⚖️ Asks for trade-offs and risks

See the difference:

❌ Weak Prompt"What is better, Microsoft Copilot or Google Gemini for a company?"
✅ Strong Prompt"You are advising the CEO of a global athletic shoe company with mixed Microsoft and Google teams. Recommend ONE platform based on their priorities: e-commerce, marketing, and 42-country security. Include risks."
"You are advising the CEO of a global company choosing between Microsoft Copilot and Google Gemini. Company context: StrideX Global — 3,200 employees, 42 countries, mixed Microsoft and Google tool usage. Top priorities: e-commerce performance and marketing effectiveness. Budget: mid-tier enterprise plan. Deadline: board presentation in one week. Your task: Recommend ONE platform. Give your top 3 reasons. Then tell me the biggest risk of your recommendation and one thing that could make you change your mind."

Before running it — check all 5 boxes:

  • Forces the AI to pick ONE platform (not "it depends")
  • Includes the company's name and size
  • Mentions StrideX's top business priorities
  • Asks for at least one risk or downside
  • Has specific context (42 countries, mixed teams, deadline)

✏️ Your Refined Prompt

The best prompts sound like you're briefing a smart consultant who knows nothing about StrideX yet.

SECTION 05 OF 06

Executive Decision Brief

This is your final deliverable. Write a one-page brief the CEO can actually use — clear, sharp, and honest about what you know and don't know.

⏱ 15–20 minutes

🧾 What Is an Executive Decision Brief?

A short document for a busy leader who doesn't have time to read pages of analysis. It gives them: the recommendation, the reasons, the risks, and the honest caveats — all in one page or less.

Think of it like a text message to a CEO: get to the point, be honest, make it easy to act on. No fluff. No "it depends." Just your best, clearest thinking.

✍️ Executive Writing Rules

  • Short sentences. Active voice. ("We recommend X" not "It is recommended that X be considered.")
  • No filler phrases like "In today's fast-paced business world..." — just say what you mean
  • Every claim should be tied to something specific about StrideX
  • Include bad news too — CEOs don't trust advisors who hide the downsides
